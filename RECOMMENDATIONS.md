# What You Can Actually Do: Evidence-Based Recommendations

**Last verified: March 2026 | Every recommendation below is backed by human RCT or large prospective cohort data.**

This is not a supplement list. This is not biohacker speculation. These are the interventions with the strongest published evidence for slowing biological aging, reducing all-cause mortality, and extending healthspan — things you can start today, for free or near-free.

Recommendations are ordered by evidence strength. Each includes specific doses, frequencies, and effect sizes from the data.

---

## Tier 1: Strongest Evidence (Large RCTs and/or Meta-Analyses)

### 1. Cardiorespiratory Fitness (VO2 Max)

**VO2 max is the single strongest predictor of longevity in the entire medical literature.** Nothing else — no drug, no supplement, no diet — comes close to these effect sizes.

**The Cleveland Clinic study** (Mandsager et al. 2018, n=122,007, 8.4-year follow-up, PMID: 30646252):
- Elite fitness (top 2.3%) vs. Low fitness (bottom 25%): **HR 0.20** — an **80% lower risk of death**
- Low fitness mortality penalty (HR 5.04) **exceeded smoking (HR 1.41), diabetes (HR 1.40), and coronary artery disease (HR 1.29)**
- **No ceiling effect observed.** Mortality decreased monotonically through the elite tier. Being extremely fit is never harmful by this data.

**Meta-analysis of meta-analyses** (Lang et al. 2024, 26 reviews, >20.9M observations, PMID: 38599681):
- High vs. low CRF: **HR 0.47** (95% CI 0.39-0.56) — 53% lower all-cause mortality
- Per 1-MET increase: **HR 0.83-0.89** — each MET gained reduces mortality 11-17%

**Minimum effective dose** (Wen et al. 2011, 416,175 people, PMID: 21846575):
- As little as **15 min/day** of moderate exercise reduced mortality by **14%** and added **3 years of life expectancy**
- Each additional 15 min/day further reduced mortality by 4%

**Vigorous bouts matter** (Stamatakis et al. 2022, *Nature Medicine*, PMID: 36482104):
- Even **4.4 min/day** of vigorous incidental activity (stairs, brisk walking) in non-exercisers: **26-30% lower all-cause mortality, 32-34% lower CVD mortality**

**Zone 2 vs. HIIT**: No head-to-head mortality RCT exists. Both reduce mortality. HIIT improves VO2 max faster per minute invested. Zone 2 has the deepest long-term epidemiological data. Best approach: do both.

**Recommendation**: This is the #1 priority. Build VO2 max with 3-4 sessions/week combining:
- Zone 2 cardio (conversation-pace sustained effort): 150+ min/week
- VO2 max intervals (near-max effort, 4x4 min or similar): 1-2x/week
- Even 15 min/day is meaningful if you're currently sedentary. Any increase in fitness reduces mortality with no upper limit.

### 2. Resistance Training

Resistance training is additive to cardio — the combination produces the largest mortality reduction of any exercise pattern.

**Any resistance training vs. none** (Shailendra et al. 2022, PMID: 35599175):
- All-cause mortality: **RR 0.85** (15% reduction)
- Cancer mortality: **RR 0.86** (14% reduction)
- **Optimal dose: ~60 min/week → RR 0.74** (27% reduction). J-shaped curve — benefits plateau then may reverse above ~90 min/week.

**Resistance + aerobic vs. neither** (Saeidifard et al. 2019, Mayo Clinic, PMID: 31104484):
- **HR 0.60** — a **40% lower all-cause mortality**. This is the combination effect.

**Grip strength predicts mortality** (García-Hermoso et al. 2018, 1.9M participants, PMID: 29425700):
- Higher vs. lower grip strength: **HR 0.69** (31% lower mortality)
- Per 5-kg decrease in grip: **HR 1.16** (Wu et al. 2017, 3M participants, PMID: 28549705)

**Recommendation**: 2-3 sessions/week, ~20-30 min each (~40-60 min/week total). Focus on compound movements (squat, deadlift, press, row, pull-up patterns). The goal is preserving muscle mass and strength — grip strength alone is one of the strongest mortality predictors in the literature.

### 3. Sleep

Sleep is not passive recovery — it's an active biological maintenance process. The glymphatic system clears metabolic waste (amyloid-beta, tau) during NREM sleep, and sleep disorders are among the strongest accelerators of biological aging.

**Duration and mortality** (Ungvari et al. 2025, 79 cohort studies, PMID: 40072785):
- Short sleep (<7h): **HR 1.14** (+14% all-cause mortality)
- Long sleep (≥9h): **HR 1.34** (+34% mortality, though partly reverse causation)
- **Optimal: 7-8 hours/night**

**Sleep regularity is independently lethal** (Kalkanis et al. 2025, 59 studies, PMID: 41259946):
- Irregular sleepers: **20-88% higher all-cause mortality**, independent of duration and quality
- Also: 26-53% higher dementia risk

**Sleep affects biological aging by epigenetic clocks** (Diao et al. 2025, n=3,566, PMID: 40442824):
- Better sleep score → lower GrimAgeAccel and PhenoAgeAccel
- **DunedinPACE mediated 6.2% of the sleep → lower mortality association** — first study linking sleep to mortality partially via pace-of-aging
- Mendelian randomization confirms: **insomnia causally accelerates GrimAge** (Zhao et al. 2025, PMID: 40032851)

**Sleep apnea is the biggest sleep-aging accelerator** — intermittent hypoxia creates repeated ischemia-reperfusion injury:
- Telomere shortening: **SMD -0.451** (meta-analysis of 49 studies, Chang et al. 2026, PMID: 41655394)
- Most consistent predictor of GrimAge and DunedinPACE acceleration across all sleep disorders (Autio et al. 2025, PMID: 40176161)
- Glymphatic impairment directly imaged in untreated OSA patients (Keil et al. 2026, PMID: 41713702)

**Glymphatic system**: Partially validated in humans via new MRI techniques (Hirschler et al. 2025, *Nature Neuroscience*, PMID: 41087750). Clears amyloid-beta, tau, and metabolic waste during NREM sleep. Full causal human data is ~3-5 years away.

**What to do** (ranked by evidence):
1. **Get screened for sleep apnea** if you snore or wake unrefreshed — CPAP has the largest biological aging effect size of any sleep intervention
2. **Keep a consistent wake time** — regularity independently predicts mortality
3. **CBT-I for chronic insomnia** — RCT evidence that it reverses inflammatory aging markers (IL-6, TNF-α) over 15 months (Irwin et al. 2024, PMID: 38777285). First-line over sleep medications.
4. **Target 7-8 hours** — avoid chronic short sleeping
5. **Exercise** — best behavioral enhancer of sleep architecture
6. **Cool bedroom (65-68°F)** — promotes deep NREM. Mechanistic support; no aging RCT data.

### 4. Diet

The strongest dietary evidence points to a Mediterranean-style, high-fiber, plant-forward pattern — not any single food or macro ratio.

**Mediterranean diet** (Eleftheriou et al. 2018, 30 cohorts, 225,600 deaths, PMID: 30401007):
- Highest vs. lowest adherence: **HR 0.79** (95% CI 0.77-0.81) — 21% mortality reduction
- Driven by: moderate fruit/vegetables, legumes, whole grains, olive oil, fish, low red meat

**Dietary fiber** (Ramezani et al. 2024, 64 cohorts, 3.5M subjects, PMID: 38011755):
- High vs. low fiber: **HR 0.77** (95% CI 0.73-0.82) — 23% mortality reduction
- Fiber from nuts/seeds for CVD mortality: **HR 0.57** — a 43% reduction
- Insoluble fiber more effective than soluble for mortality

**Protein — the age-dependent paradox** (Levine/Longo 2014, NHANES, PMID: 24606898):
- **Ages 50-65**: High protein (≥20% calories) → **75% higher all-cause mortality**, 4x cancer mortality. Driven by IGF-1/mTOR activation. Effect abolished if protein was plant-derived.
- **Ages 65+**: The relationship **reverses**. High protein becomes protective against cancer and all-cause mortality (sarcopenia/frailty prevention dominates).
- For older adults: 1.0-1.6 g/kg/day supported for muscle preservation, especially with resistance training. Plant protein attenuates mTOR concerns.

**Ultra-processed foods accelerate biological aging** (Wang et al. 2025, UK Biobank, n=172,225, PMID: 40719960):
- Highest vs. lowest UPF: **HR 1.15** (95% CI 1.07-1.23). PhenoAge acceleration mediated 14.4% of the effect.
- Each 10% increment of UPF in diet: **HR 1.10** (PMID: 40033461)

**Omega-3 / DHA** (O'Keefe et al. 2024, UK Biobank + 17 cohorts, 160K subjects, PMID: 38506781):
- Highest vs. lowest plasma DHA: **HR 0.79** (95% CI 0.74-0.85) — 21% mortality reduction

**Blue Zones reality check** (Candal-Pedreira et al. 2025, PMID: 40479568): Only Ogliastra, Okinawa, and Nicoya have strong scientific verification. Loma Linda has **no published scientific studies** despite being the most cited. Common threads across verified zones: high legumes, high vegetables, low animal protein, low ultra-processed food.

**Recommendation**: Eat mostly plants — legumes, vegetables, whole grains, nuts, olive oil, fish. Minimize ultra-processed food. Get 25-30g+ fiber daily from diverse sources. Under 65: keep protein moderate and plant-forward. Over 65: increase protein to 1.2-1.6 g/kg (sarcopenia prevention outweighs mTOR concern). Don't chase superfoods — the pattern matters more than any single ingredient.

### 5. Don't Smoke / Quit Smoking

Smoking accelerates every hallmark of aging simultaneously — DNA damage, telomere shortening, epigenetic aging, inflammation, senescence, mitochondrial dysfunction. If you smoke, quitting is the single highest-impact intervention available.

- Current active smokers show significant epigenetic age acceleration across multiple clocks (Perez-Garcia et al. 2025, PMID: 41445615)
- **Intergenerational effect**: Paternal smoking accelerates biological aging *in newborns* — telomere shortening documented in offspring (Stagnar et al. 2026, PMID: 41232904)
- Cessation shows partial recovery in epigenetic age markers — it's never too late to quit
- Secondhand smoke exposure in childhood accelerates epigenetic aging (Patil et al. 2025, PMID: 41281718)

### 6. Minimize or Eliminate Alcohol

**"Moderate drinking is protective" has been decisively refuted.** The J-curve was an artifact of confounding (sick quitter bias). Zero is the optimal amount.

**Mendelian randomization** (genetic instruments that remove confounding, Kassaw et al. 2024, 278,093 UK Biobank participants, PMID: 38508868):
- **Linear association** with mortality — no safe threshold, no J-curve
- All-cause mortality: **OR 1.27** (95% CI 1.16-1.39) per standard unit increase
- CVD mortality: OR 1.30; Cancer mortality: OR 1.20

**2023 JAMA meta-analysis** (Zhao et al., 107 studies, PMID: 37000449):
- After adjusting for former-drinker bias, occasional and low-volume drinkers showed **no mortality benefit** vs. lifetime non-drinkers
- Female drinkers had **significantly higher** mortality risk (RR 1.22)

**Alcohol accelerates biological aging** (Wang et al. 2023, Framingham Heart Study, PMID: 37889500):
- One additional drink/day = **+0.71 years PhenoAge acceleration** in middle-aged adults
- Liquor worst (+0.82 years GrimAge), wine and beer slightly less
- Telomere shortening: 10→32 units/week = **equivalent of 3 years of biological aging** (Topiwala et al. 2022, MR study, PMID: 35879401)

**WHO/Lancet 2023-2025 consensus**: "The level of consumption that minimises risk is **0 g of ethanol per week**." Alcohol is a Group 1 carcinogen (same category as asbestos, radiation, tobacco).

**Recommendation**: The optimal amount of alcohol for longevity is zero. Every drink accelerates biological aging measurably. If you drink, less is always better.

## Tier 2: Strong Observational Evidence + Mechanistic Support

### 7. Social Connection and Purpose

This is not soft advice. The effect sizes rival smoking and obesity.

**Social isolation and mortality** (Wang et al. 2023, 90 cohort studies, 2.2 million participants, PMID: 37337095):
- Social isolation: **HR 1.32** (95% CI 1.26-1.39) for all-cause mortality
- Loneliness: **HR 1.14** (95% CI 1.08-1.20) for all-cause mortality
- Social isolation + breast cancer: **HR 1.51** (95% CI 1.34-1.70)

**Strong social relationships are protective** (Holt-Lunstad et al. 2010, 148 studies, 308,849 participants, PMID: 20668659):
- Complex social integration: **OR 1.91** (95% CI 1.63-2.23) — a 91% increased survival probability
- This effect size **exceeds typical smoking and obesity estimates**

**Purpose in life** (Cohen et al. 2016, meta-analysis, 136,265 participants, PMID: 26630073):
- All-cause mortality: **RR 0.83** (95% CI 0.75-0.91) — 17% reduced mortality risk
- Cardiovascular events: **RR 0.83** (95% CI 0.75-0.92)

**Recommendation**: Invest in relationships. Find or maintain purpose. This is not optional for longevity — isolation kills at rates comparable to a pack-a-day habit.

### 8. Cognitive Engagement

Continued cognitive activity is one of the strongest modifiable factors for dementia prevention.

**Cognitive leisure activities and dementia** (Yates et al. 2016, meta-analysis, PMID: 27502691):
- **HR 0.58** (95% CI 0.46-0.74) — 42% reduced dementia risk from cognitive activities

**Low education is the #1 modifiable dementia risk factor** (Stephan et al. 2024, *Lancet Healthy Longev*, PMID: 38824956):
- Population attributable fraction: **17.2%** of dementia cases attributable to low education (unweighted)
- Seven combined modifiable factors (education, hypertension, obesity, smoking, inactivity, depression, diabetes) account for 55% of dementia cases

**Retirement and cognitive decline** (Takeuchi et al. 2024, JAGES, n=48,221, PMID: 39802220):
- Continued employment vs. retirement: **OR 0.45** for dementia — though reverse causation (early decline → early retirement) cannot be excluded

**Bilingualism** (Li & Coretta 2026, Bayesian meta-analysis, PMID: 41006960):
- Bilinguals: **3.45 years later** symptom onset (95% CrI: 2.8-4.1). Replicated. But doesn't prevent incidence — delays onset only.

**Social isolation's cognitive harm is direct, not emotional** (Hale et al. 2026, causal inference study, n=30,421, PMID: 41399016):
- Only 6% of isolation's cognitive effect operates through loneliness — the rest is structural/direct

**Recommendation**: Stay cognitively engaged — work, volunteer, learn languages, solve problems. The evidence doesn't favor any specific brain training product over genuine engagement with complex activities. Continued productive activity (paid or volunteer) after traditional retirement age shows the strongest associations.

### 9. Stress Management

Chronic stress accelerates biological aging through inflammation and telomere shortening.

- **Caregiving stress → telomere shortening**: Chronic stress drives NF-κB, IL-1β, IL-6 expression, which predicts telomere attrition over 15 months (Lin et al. 2018, PMID: 29980010)
- **Meditation preserves telomeres**: Loving-kindness meditation RCT (12 weeks, n=142) showed significantly less telomere attrition vs. controls (Le Nguyen et al. 2019, PMID: 31185369). 18-month Age-Well trial (n=137, 65+) showed meditation attendance correlated with telomere preservation (r=0.45, Kaliman et al. 2024, PMID: 39582797).
- **Exercise reverses stress-related telomere shortening** in caregivers (Puterman et al. 2018, PMID: 30266522)
- **Mindfulness reverses stress-induced epigenetic changes** (Househam 2023, PMID: 36863798)

**Recommendation**: Any consistent stress-reduction practice — meditation, yoga, therapy, time in nature. The mechanism (cortisol → inflammation → telomere shortening) is well-established. Exercise doubles as both fitness and stress management.

### 10. Gut Health

Gut microbiome diversity declines with age and correlates with longevity. Centenarians have distinct microbiome profiles across multiple cultures (Italian, Chinese, Japanese, Sardinian cohorts).

- **Mendelian randomization** now supports **causal** (not just correlational) links between specific microbiome features and longevity (Liu et al. 2023, PMID: 36991009)
- **Akkermansia muciniphila**: First human RCT (pasteurized strain, 12 weeks) improved muscle strength in sarcopenia-prone older adults (Kang et al. 2024, PMID: 39683431). Promising but early.
- **Mediterranean diet increases microbiome diversity** and Akkermansia abundance (Lauria et al. 2026, PMID: 41381307)
- **Diversity of fiber sources** matters more than absolute dose — plant-based variety is key (Beke & Dahl 2026, PMID: 40459489)

**Recommendation**: Eat diverse plant fiber (vegetables, legumes, whole grains, nuts, seeds). Include fermented foods (yogurt, kimchi, sauerkraut). The Mediterranean diet pattern consistently improves gut microbiome profiles.

### 11. Oral Health

Periodontal disease drives systemic inflammation through the same pathways (NF-κB, IL-6, TNF-α) that fuel inflammaging.

- Tooth loss and periodontal disease associated with all-cause and CVD mortality in UK/USA cohorts over 9-15 year follow-up (Kotronia et al. 2021, PMID: 34385519)
- Periodontal pathogens enter the bloodstream and trigger endothelial dysfunction (Foroughi & Parang 2026, PMID: 41851576)
- Periodontitis significantly associated with mortality in elderly >75 years (Qi et al. 2020, PMID: 32020486)

**Recommendation**: Brush, floss, get regular dental care. Periodontal disease is a treatable, avoidable source of chronic systemic inflammation.

## Tier 3: Promising but Less Certain

### 12. Heat Exposure (Sauna)

The Finnish sauna data is striking but may be partially confounded by healthy-user bias.

**Kuopio Ischemic Heart Disease Study** (Laukkanen et al. 2015, 2,315 men, 20.7-year follow-up, PMID: 25705824):
- 4-7x/week vs 1x/week: **HR 0.37** (95% CI 0.18-0.75) for sudden cardiac death
- 2-3x/week: **HR 0.71** (95% CI 0.52-0.98) for all-cause mortality
- Duration >19 min/session: **HR 0.48** (95% CI 0.31-0.75) for sudden cardiac death

**Gender-inclusive cohort** (2018, 1,688 participants, 15-year follow-up, PMID: 30486813):
- 4-7x/week: **HR 0.23** (95% CI 0.08-0.65) for CVD mortality after full adjustment

**Causality question**: Associations survived adjustment for physical activity, SES, and CVD risk factors. Sauna frequency independently improved cardiorespiratory fitness (+0.84-1.17 mL/kg/min, Kunutsor 2024, PMID: 38836690). Likely partially causal, partially confounded.

**Recommendation**: If accessible, 2-3+ sessions/week at 80-90°C for 15-20 minutes. Not a substitute for exercise. Finnish-style dry sauna is the studied modality — infrared saunas have less data.

### 13. Cold Exposure

**No human mortality or longevity data exists.** Zero prospective studies on intentional cold exposure and lifespan.

- Cold water immersion studies are limited to acute biomarkers and recovery
- Brown fat activation is established but not linked to human lifespan
- Cold-related excess winter mortality in elderly argues for caution, not enthusiasm
- A 2025 review (PMID: 39884345) examines theoretical mechanisms but acknowledges the absence of human longevity data

**Recommendation**: No evidence-based recommendation possible. Cold exposure is not harmful in moderation for healthy individuals, but claims about longevity benefits are unsupported by human data.

### 14. Sun Exposure / Vitamin D

A surprising finding: **vitamin D supplements don't help, but sun avoidance may kill**.

**Vitamin D supplementation — RCTs are negative:**
- VITAL trial (largest RCT): No mortality benefit from 2000 IU/day vitamin D3
- D-Health trial (21,315 older adults, PMID: 35026158): No reduction in all-cause mortality
- 2026 meta-analysis (PMID: 41719624): When confounding is removed through randomization, vitamin D benefits disappear
- Relationship is non-linear — increased risk at LOW levels, limited benefit beyond sufficiency (Dalamaga et al. 2026, PMID: 41849024)

**Sun avoidance — as dangerous as smoking:**
- Swedish cohort (Lindqvist et al. 2016, PMID: 26992108): **Women who avoided sun had life expectancy similar to smokers.** 0.6-2.1 years reduced life expectancy.
- **UK Biobank replication** (Stevenson et al. 2024, PMID: 39094281): UV exposure inversely associated with all-cause, CVD, and cancer mortality. Confirms Swedish finding in different population.

**Recommendation**: Get regular moderate sun exposure. Don't supplement vitamin D unless deficient (get tested). Don't avoid the sun entirely — the mortality data suggests sun avoidance is a greater risk than sun exposure for most people. This does not mean ignore skin cancer risk — it means balance.

## What to Avoid: Biggest Aging Accelerators

Based on epigenetic clock and biomarker data, these are the largest avoidable accelerators of biological aging:

| Accelerator | Effect | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Smoking** | Strongest and most consistent epigenetic age acceleration across all clocks. Intergenerational effects on offspring. | PMID: 41445615, 41232904 |
| **Social isolation** | HR 1.32 for all-cause mortality (2.2M participants). Effect size rivals smoking. | PMID: 37337095 |
| **Air pollution (PM2.5)** | +0.74 years epigenetic age per 5 μg/m³ in adults (meta-analysis trend, not yet statistically significant). | PMID: 41564677 |
| **Pesticide exposure** | +1.72 years epigenetic age acceleration from herbicides (Lifelines cohort). Occupational hazard. | PMID: 41271129 |
| **Heavy metal exposure** | Chronic Pb/Hg/Cd mixture triggers premature brain aging and telomere shortening. | PMID: 40393628, 39068742 |
| **Chronic stress** | Drives NF-κB/IL-6 inflammation → telomere shortening. Caregivers show accelerated cellular aging. | PMID: 29980010 |
| **Periodontal disease** | Chronic source of systemic IL-6/TNF-α. Associated with CVD and all-cause mortality. | PMID: 34385519 |

## What NOT to Bother With (Evidence Doesn't Support)

Based on our verification work, the following popular interventions lack evidence of functional benefit in humans:

| Intervention | Why Not |
|---|---|
| NMN/NR supplements | Two meta-analyses: zero functional benefits. NAD+ goes up; nothing measurable improves. |
| Resveratrol | Original SIRT1 activation was an assay artifact. GSK program failed. ~1% bioavailability. |
| Fisetin supplements | Unreplicated mouse study. Only human study (n=10) was negative. Not ITP-tested. |
| Metformin (if you exercise) | Blunts exercise adaptations (VO2 max, mitochondrial respiration). May do net harm. |
| GDF11 anything | Debunked. Exacerbates cardiac injury. |
| Growth hormone for anti-aging | TRIIM was n=9 no controls. GH/IGF-1 paradox: reduced GH extends lifespan in every model organism. Increases cancer risk. |
| Unregulated stem cell treatments | 35 documented adverse events including blindness and death. No efficacy proven. |
