# Regulatory and Ethical Landscape of Aging Research

**Last verified: March 2026 | Sources: PubMed, FDA.gov, federal court records**

This section covers the laws, regulations, ethical debates, and institutional structures that shape what aging interventions get tested, approved, and made available — and why the evidence landscape looks the way it does.

---

## The Core Problem: Aging Is Not a Disease

The FDA has never approved a drug with "aging" as the indication. Under current FDA statute, drugs must treat, mitigate, cure, or prevent a defined "disease or condition." Aging is classified as a normal biological process, not a disease. This single regulatory fact has cascading consequences:

- **Pharma won't fund aging trials** — no FDA-approvable indication means no return on investment
- **Generic drugs can't get trial funding** — rapamycin and metformin have no patent, so no company will spend $100M+ on trials they can't recoup
- **Every aging drug must disguise itself as a disease drug** — senolytics target osteoarthritis, rapamycin targets Alzheimer's, metformin targets "multimorbidity"
- **Supplement companies fill the vacuum** — DSHEA lets them sell NMN/fisetin/resveratrol without proving efficacy, so the market fills with unproven products
- **The evidence stays weak** — small, underfunded trials by tiny companies miss primary endpoints, and we conclude "it doesn't work" when really "it was never properly tested"

> **This reframes the entire findings table.** "LOW evidence" often means "never properly tested" — not "doesn't work."

### The TAME Trial as Regulatory Strategy

The TAME trial (Targeting Aging with Metformin) is as much a regulatory play as a clinical trial. Barzilai designed it specifically to create an FDA precedent:

- **Primary endpoint is multimorbidity** — time to first new diagnosis of CVD, cancer, dementia, or death — avoiding the need to claim "aging" as an indication
- **FDA granted an IND exemption** — the first time FDA formally engaged with a trial targeting aging biology as its mechanism
- If TAME succeeds, it creates a **regulatory template**: drugs could be labeled for preventing/delaying age-related multimorbidity, which functions as a proxy for "targeting aging"

Citations: Barzilai et al. 2016 *Cell Metab* (PMID: 27304507); Justice et al. 2016 *J Gerontol A* (PMID: 27535966); Justice et al. 2018 *Geroscience* (PMID: 30151729)

### WHO ICD-11: The Compromise

WHO did add aging-related codes to ICD-11 — but as a compromise, not a full disease classification:

- **MG2A**: Originally "Old age," renamed in 2022 to "Ageing-associated decline in intrinsic capacity." A primary code under "General Symptoms."
- **XT9T**: An extension code ("Ageing-related") allowing aging to be tagged as a causal/risk factor. Cannot stand alone — must modify a primary diagnosis.

The key paper driving this: Khaltourina et al. 2020 (*Mechanisms of Ageing and Development*, PMID: 32251691) applied WHO's own disease criteria to biological aging and showed it meets all of them. Brazil's geriatrics community opposed the change with the #VelhiceNãoÉDoença ("Old Age is Not a Disease") campaign, arguing it would reinforce ageism.

**What XT9T enables**: Epidemiological tracking, insurance coding of aging-related conditions, and a foothold for drug development. **What it doesn't do**: Classify aging as a primary disease. The FDA is not bound by ICD-11 codes for its approval framework.

### The Patent Problem: Why the Best Drugs Can't Get Trials

The most evidence-based longevity drug candidates (rapamycin, metformin, acarbose) are all **off-patent generics**. This creates a structural market failure:

- **No patent = no pharma incentive** — a company cannot recoup $100M+ in trial costs for a drug anyone can manufacture
- **TAME has been stuck for a decade** because no pharma company will fund a metformin aging trial. It relies on NIA, Hevolution Foundation, and philanthropic donors.
- **PEARL was funded by AgelessRx** — a tiny longevity medicine company where all authors are employees/shareholders
- **Alternative funding models**: NIA grants, Hevolution Foundation (Saudi PIF-backed), Cures Within Reach (nonprofit generic repurposing), philanthropic foundations. All undersized relative to need.

**The perverse result**: The safest, cheapest, most evidence-based drugs (generics) lack commercial incentive for new indication trials, while the least regulated, least tested compounds (supplements) face zero barriers to market entry.

### EU: Stricter on Supplements, Similar Gaps on Aging

The EU's ATMP (Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products) framework differs from FDA:
- **Slower**: EMA requires larger clinical datasets; 18-24+ month review vs. FDA's expedited RMAT pathway
- **GMO requirement**: All in vivo gene therapies require GMO approval in EU — an additional barrier
- **Hospital exemption** (Article 28): Theoretically allows patient-specific ATMPs without marketing authorization, but no explicit guidance for aging therapies
- **24+ ATMPs approved** in EU since 2009 — **zero with aging/longevity indication**
- **EMA does not recognize aging as a valid indication**, same as FDA

**Key difference on supplements**: EU's EFSA requires pre-market authorization for health claims with a >95% rejection rate. "Healthy aging," "anti-aging," and "promotes longevity" claims are **prohibited** in the EU. NMN and quercetin failed EFSA authorization but are freely marketed in the US. This creates a 5-10x larger longevity supplement market in the US — DSHEA is an American phenomenon.

### No Aging Biomarker Has Been FDA-Qualified

Despite strong validation data (GrimAge2 HR=2.57 for mortality; DunedinPACE strongest single mortality predictor), **no epigenetic clock or aging biomarker has been formally submitted to FDA for biomarker qualification** as of March 2026.

Under the FDA's Biomarker Qualification Program (21st Century Cures Act), a submission would require analytical validity, biological plausibility, clinical validation, RCT responsiveness, and defined context of use. DunedinPACE meets criteria 1-4 (CALERIE trial showed RCT responsiveness) but no Biomarker Qualification Submission has been filed.

This is a critical bottleneck: without a qualified surrogate endpoint, every aging trial must wait for hard clinical outcomes (disease, death) — requiring thousands of participants and decades of follow-up.

Citation: Niedernhofer et al. 2017 *Ageing Res Rev* (PMID: 27721062); Waziry et al. 2023 *Nat Aging* (PMID: 37118425)

## The Supplement Loophole: DSHEA

The Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 (DSHEA) created the regulatory environment that allows the $6+ billion US longevity supplement market to exist:

- **No pre-market approval required** — manufacturers can sell without demonstrating safety or efficacy
- **FDA must prove unsafety** — the burden of proof is inverted compared to drugs
- **Structure/function claims are allowed** — "supports cellular energy" is legal; "treats aging" is not, but the line is thin
- **Quality control is voluntary** — no required testing for purity, dosing accuracy, or contamination. FDA inspections find inadequate specifications, underdosed products, and contamination with heavy metals, pesticides, and undeclared pharmaceutical agents.

### The NMN Ban-Then-Reversal

In 2022-2023, FDA determined NMN was not a lawful supplement because MetroBiotech had already filed it as an Investigational New Drug. In September 2025, FDA reversed course, declaring NMN can be sold as a supplement because it was marketed as one before the drug investigation began. NMN is now classified as a New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) requiring premarket notification.

This whipsaw illustrates the regulatory confusion: the same molecule is simultaneously a supplement you can buy on Amazon and a drug candidate in clinical trials.

### Why This Matters for Evidence Quality

**DSHEA removes the incentive to generate evidence.** If you can sell NMN at scale without proving it works, why spend $50M on an RCT that might show it doesn't? This is why:
- NMN has 12 RCTs totaling 513 participants, mostly small and industry-funded
- Two meta-analyses found zero functional benefits
- The global NMN market continues to grow regardless

The business model rewards marketing, not science.

## Stem Cells: The Regulatory Battlefield

### The Scale of the Problem

By 2016, approximately **570 businesses across 700+ clinic locations** in the US were advertising unapproved stem cell treatments (Knoepfler & Turner 2018, PMID: 29327974). From 2009 to 2014, the number doubled every year. FDA warning letters during this period numbered in the single digits — a "problematic disconnect."

### The 351 vs. 361 Legal Gateway

The central regulatory question for any cell therapy: is it a Section 361 HCT/P (minimal regulation) or a Section 351 biologic (full BLA required)?

**Section 361 exemption requires ALL four criteria:**
1. Minimal manipulation (no enzymatic digestion, expansion, or culture)
2. Homologous use (cells perform the same function as in the donor)
3. Not combined with drugs or devices
4. Not for systemic effect

Most clinic-administered stem cell products fail these criteria. Injecting fat-derived cells into eyes, spines, or bloodstreams is neither "minimal manipulation" nor "homologous use."

### Documented Harms

**At least 35 documented adverse event cases** from unregulated stem cell treatments, including deaths, tumors, blindness, and severe infections (Bauer et al. 2018, PMID: 30063299):

| Harm | Cases | Details |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Bilateral blindness** | 3 patients | AMD patients at US Stem Cell Inc., Florida. Paid $5,000 each. All lost vision permanently. (Kuriyan et al. 2017, *NEJM*, PMID: 28296617) |
| **Deaths** | 6+ documented | Intracranial hemorrhage (Xcell Center, Germany), cardiac arrest (Dominican Republic), pulmonary embolism (RNL Bio, Japan), hypovolemic shock (Macquarie, Australia) |
| **Brain/spinal tumors** | 3 cases | Glioneural neoplasms after fetal neural SC injection (Moscow, China, Portugal) |
| **Bacterial infections** | 20 patients | Contaminated cord blood products in 8 US states. 65% of product vials contaminated. E. coli in 14 patients. CDC/FDA investigation. (Hartnett et al. 2021, *JAMA Network Open*, PMID: 34618037) |

### US Stem Cell Inc. v. FDA — The Landmark Case

The FDA filed for permanent injunction against US Stem Cell Inc. (the clinic that blinded three AMD patients). In 2019, the Southern District of Florida ruled for the FDA. In **July 2021, the 11th Circuit Court of Appeals unanimously upheld** the injunction, establishing that SVF products are unapproved biologics requiring BLA approval.

This killed the "it's autologous so it's exempt" argument — at least in the 11th Circuit. Clinics in other circuits remain in legal ambiguity.

### Exosome Therapy: The Next Wave

After the SVF crackdown, the market shifted to exosomes (extracellular vesicles). In **August 2019**, FDA issued a safety alert after 12 patients developed serious infections (Serratia marcescens bacteremia) from IV exosome injections at a Nebraska clinic. FDA treats exosomes identically to stem cells: unapproved biologics requiring BLA. **No exosome product has FDA approval for any indication.**

### The Ethics of Stem Cell Types

| Type | Ethical Status | Legal Status | Key Issue |
|------|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Embryonic (hESC)** | Contested — requires embryo destruction | Dickey-Wicker Amendment (1996): bans federal funding for embryo destruction. Research on *already-derived* lines is funded. Renewed annually for 30 years. | Fundamental moral disagreement on embryo personhood |
| **iPSC (Yamanaka)** | Largely resolved — no embryo involved | Fully fundable; no Dickey-Wicker restriction | Remaining concerns: tumorigenicity, immunogenicity, epigenetic memory |
| **Adult/autologous** | Ethically uncontroversial in principle | Gray area when used for unapproved indications; 351 vs 361 question | Exploited by unregulated clinics |

### Informed Consent Is Structurally Compromised

For-profit stem cell clinics systematically undermine informed consent:
- **ClinicalTrials.gov exploitation**: Clinics registered sham studies on the government database to give patients the impression of scientific legitimacy. Patients saw the .gov URL and interpreted it as endorsement. (Kuriyan 2017; Knoepfler/Turner 2018)
- **Desperate patients cannot fully separate rational risk assessment from hope** — the concept of "informed hope" (Reimer et al. 2010, PMID: 20461643) describes this structural limitation
- **Financial model**: Patients pay $5,000-$50,000 out of pocket for unproven treatments, creating a perverse incentive where the sickest and most desperate are most exploited

## Stem Cell Tourism and Medical Tourism

### Where Americans Go

Major destinations for unregulated stem cell and regenerative treatments: Mexico, Panama, Cayman Islands, Colombia, Thailand, China, India, Ukraine, Germany, Costa Rica.

### Documented Harm from Tourism

- Complication rates from stem cell medical tourism as high as **56%** (Foley et al. 2019, PMID: 31281926)
- South Africa SCI study: patients who traveled for stem cell therapy showed **no improvement** in functional or neurological scores (Skeen et al. 2019, PMID: 31662144)
- Asia accounts for 34% of reported complications from unregulated aesthetic therapies including exosomes and stem cell products (Rahman et al. 2025, PMID: 40593115)

## International Regulatory Comparison

### Japan: Speed Over Evidence

Japan's 2014 Act on Safety of Regenerative Medicine created the world's first conditional/time-limited approval system:

- Products can reach insured patients with **only preliminary safety and efficacy data**
- Conditional approval granted for up to 7 years; full approval requires post-marketing evidence
- **21 regenerative medical products authorized** as of 2024 (vs. 17 by FDA as of 2019)
- World firsts in 2026: iPS-derived therapies for heart failure (ReHeart) and Parkinson's (Amchepry)

**The tradeoff is real:**
- HeartSheet (cardiac cell sheets) approved on evidence from **only 7 patients without a primary efficacy endpoint** — then **withdrawn in 2024** after failing to demonstrate efficacy
- Collategene (gene therapy for limb ischemia) — also withdrawn after efficacy failure
- Adverse event reporting is **333x lower** under Japan's regenerative medicine act vs. standard drug regulation — suggesting massive underreporting, not greater safety (from published comparison data)

### China: Governance Gaps

The He Jiankui CRISPR baby case (2018) exposed systemic issues:
- Ethics governance depends on **scientist self-regulation rather than enforcement**
- Weak IRB structures lacking independence
- Clinical trials advancing faster than safety data accumulation
- Post-He regulatory reforms enacted but enforcement remains uncertain

China's NMPA appears more permissive than FDA for regenerative medicine, with less restrictive pre-clinical documentation requirements.

### Right-to-Try: A Dead End for Aging

The 2018 federal Right to Try Act requires a **terminal diagnosis**. Aging does not qualify. No documented attempt to use right-to-try for a longevity intervention has been found. This pathway is structurally unavailable for anti-aging therapies.

### Off-Label Prescribing: Legal but Lonely

- Off-label prescribing of FDA-approved drugs (rapamycin, metformin) for aging is **legal** (Syed et al. 2021, PMID: 33234538)
- The ACCP formally cautioned against off-label rapamycin for aging (Mallikaarjun et al. 2026, PMID: 41065325)
- Only **333 adults** documented as actively using off-label rapamycin for healthspan in the literature (Kaeberlein et al. 2023, PMID: 37191826)
- **No insurance coverage** for off-label longevity prescriptions — patients pay out of pocket
- No documented medical board actions against longevity prescribers

## The Bioethics Debate

### Treatment vs. Enhancement

If aging is treated medically, where is the line?
- Treating Alzheimer's = treatment. Preventing cognitive decline in a healthy 40-year-old = enhancement?
- **Masny 2023 (*Bioethics*, PMID: 36525613)**: Justice *requires* unconditional access to anti-aging as a "complete life" entitlement — collapsing the distinction entirely
- **Schweda & Marckmann 2013 (*Bioethics*, PMID: 22500556)**: The line should be drawn by democratic deliberation, not appeals to "naturalness"
- **McKiddy 2025 (*AMA J Ethics*, PMID: 41411401)**: Whether to prescribe gerotherapeutics depends on whether the intervention serves or exacerbates health inequalities

The regulatory system doesn't resolve this — it avoids it. FDA's refusal to classify aging as a disease is, in effect, a policy decision that aging is not a medical problem.

### Equity and Access

- **Who gets anti-aging now?** Wealthy self-experimenters (Bryan Johnson: $2M/year), off-label patients paying out of pocket, medical tourists
- **Farrelly 2022 (*HEC Forum*, PMID: 36348214)**: The "aging status quo" is already deeply unjust — wealthy people already live a decade longer. Framing anti-aging as an equity *threat* misses that the current system is the equity *emergency*.
- **Counter-concern**: No insurance coverage, no FDA approval, no public health infrastructure → longevity risks becoming a luxury good
- **Framing matters** (Aparicio 2024, PMID: 39585500): Public support for "staying healthy longer" is much higher than for "living longer." The healthspan frame resonates; the immortality frame repels.

### The "Duty to Age" vs. "Duty to Prevent Suffering"

**Bioconservative position** (Kass 2002, Fukuyama 2002, Sandel 2007):
- Mortality gives life urgency and meaning
- Human nature (including aging) grounds dignity and equal rights
- Engineering longevity is a failure to accept human limitation

**Geroscience response** (all PubMed-indexed):
- **Han & de Magalhães 2026 (*Ageing Res Rev*, PMID: 41654143)**: "The burden now falls on defenders of forced ageing to explain why preventable suffering should continue." Naturalistic fallacy (natural ≠ good). Note: de Magalhães has industry COI.
- **Wareham 2015 (*Theor Med Bioeth*, PMID: 26246312)**: Overpopulation from slowed aging is "highly unlikely" — comparable to 20th-century lifespan gains which didn't produce catastrophe
- **Cutas 2008 (*J Med Ethics*, PMID: 18757626)**: No argument about what death contributes to existence can justify preventing people from seeking to live longer
- **Wareham 2020 (PMID: 32152896)**: The "loneliness problem" — outliving your social network creates profound isolation. A practical concern, not a metaphysical one.

### Animal Research Ethics — An Unexamined Gap

The NIA ITP uses cohorts of 150-400 mice per sex per site, across three simultaneous sites, running full lifespan studies. Each compound test requires hundreds of mice living and dying over 2-3 years. The scale is substantial.

**No dedicated bioethics papers on ITP-scale longevity animal research exist in PubMed.** Oversight is entirely IACUC-procedural, not philosophically analyzed. Given the high translation failure rate (resveratrol, GDF11, and many others failed to translate to humans), the utilitarian calculus of animal use is difficult to justify prospectively. This is a genuine ethics gap the field has not confronted.

## The Longevity Dividend: What's at Stake Economically

Goldman et al. 2013 (*Health Affairs*, PMID: 24101058) used the Future Elderly Model to estimate:
- Delayed aging scenario: +2.2 years of life expectancy, mostly in good health
- **Estimated economic value: $7.1 trillion over 50 years** (2010-2060)
- Addressing cancer alone yields only +0.5 years. Heart disease alone: +0.5 years. **Delayed aging beats both combined** because aging is the common cause of all age-related disease.

The top 5% of Medicare beneficiaries consume ~50% of expenditures — overwhelmingly multi-morbid elderly (Nikolich-Žugich et al. 2016, PMID: 26419976). Healthspan extension *compresses* expensive end-of-life morbidity. Lifespan extension without healthspan extension amplifies it.

**Pension concern is solvable**: Goldman's model shows near-zero net fiscal impact if retirement age is indexed to healthy life expectancy. The 3-stage life model (education → work → retirement at 65) was designed for 61-year average lifespans in 1935.

## The Structural Thesis — Now With Receipts

The evidence landscape in aging research is not shaped primarily by the science. It's shaped by the regulatory and economic structure. **This is not speculation — it's stated explicitly in published literature, including FDA's own words.**

1. **FDA won't approve "aging"** → pharma won't fund trials → only generic/supplement companies pursue it → trials are small and underfunded → endpoints are missed → we conclude "it doesn't work in humans"
   - Newman & Barzilai 2016 (*J Gerontol A*, PMID: 27535968): foundational paper naming regulatory barriers
   - **von Haehling et al. 2025 (*J Cachexia Sarcopenia Muscle*, PMID: 40485364): At a Dec 2024 FDA workshop, FDA stated sarcopenia safety standards would match diabetes trials, making programs "so large that from an economic standpoint only therapies that significantly impact on morbidity/mortality outcomes have a chance to be considered commercially feasible."** This is FDA explicitly confirming the barrier.

2. **DSHEA lets supplements sell without proof** → no incentive for rigorous trials → NMN/fisetin/resveratrol evidence stays weak → market fills with unproven products
   - Fisetin is only now getting a rigorous RCT — by being framed as a cancer survivorship drug (Ji et al. 2026, *Ther Adv Med Oncol*, PMID: 41835341). The disease-wedge was the only way.

3. **Generic drugs have no patent incentive** → TAME stuck for a decade → PEARL funded by tiny startup with COI
   - de Grey 2019 (*Rejuvenation Res*, PMID: 31595812): "$75M from philanthropy, not pharma" — an explicit editorial on the market failure
   - resTORbio tried to solve this by creating a patentable mTOR inhibitor (RTB101). Phase 3 failed. Company collapsed. (Mannick et al. 2021, *Lancet Healthy Longev*, PMID: 33977284)
   - Kulkarni & Barzilai 2022 (*Aging Cell*, PMID: 35343051): Proposes a formal pipeline for generic drug repurposing, acknowledging the usual pharma mechanism doesn't apply.

4. **The disease-specific wedge fragments the evidence** → no trial tests "aging" directly → each disease-specific trial is small
   - Kritchevsky et al. 2025 (preprint, PMID: 39989956): 28-expert Delphi process — **no consensus exists on a unified "aging" trial endpoint**
   - Bhasin et al. 2023 (*J Gerontol A*, PMID: 37325959): FDA-participatory meeting explicitly stating "lack of regulatory guidance has impeded development of function-promoting therapies"
   - Schönstein 2024 (*Gerontologist*, PMID: 38813871): No FDA-compliant "successful aging" endpoint exists

5. **Japan's permissive system shows the speed/evidence tradeoff** — HeartSheet approved on 7 patients and later withdrawn
   - Hakariya et al. 2025 (*Clin Pharmacol Ther*, PMID: 39807768): Independent academic analysis framing HeartSheet as a cautionary tale

6. **Regulatory arbitrage fills the gap** — patients go offshore, get harmed
   - Bauer et al. 2018 (PMID: 30063299): 35 adverse events including 8+ deaths across 14 countries
   - Liz Parrish/BioViva traveled to Colombia for AAV-telomerase gene therapy to avoid FDA — no safety data published since
   - 300 US businesses still market unapproved perinatal stem cell products (Turner et al. 2024, PMID: 38340106)

**The capstone paper**: Kroemer, Ferrucci, Sierra et al. 2025 (*Cell*, PMID: 40250404) — 12 leading geroscientists including NIA's Ferrucci and Hevolution's Sierra explicitly state that gerotherapeutics will only advance "pending results from randomized clinical trials and regulatory approval" — treating **regulatory approval as the rate-limiting step**. The science is ready. The system is not.

**The uncomfortable conclusion:** We may already have interventions that slow human aging. We can't prove it because the system isn't designed to test the question.
