EU REGULATORY FRAMEWORK FOR ADVANCED THERAPY MEDICINAL PRODUCTS (ATMPs) IN AGING/LONGEVITY THERAPIES
Research compiled March 2026

================================================================================
QUESTION 1: How does the EU ATMP framework differ from FDA?
================================================================================

FINDING: FDA is significantly faster and more flexible than EMA

Key Differences:

A) Approval Pathways:
   FDA: Offers RMAT (Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy) designation plus
        Breakthrough Therapy, Fast Track, Priority Review
   EMA: Centralized review (CAT + CHMP committees) with standard 18-24 month timelines

B) Clinical Evidence Requirements:
   FDA: Accepts surrogate endpoints and real-world evidence for expedited approval
        in rare/life-threatening conditions
   EMA: Requires larger patient populations, longer monitoring periods, more
        comprehensive clinical datasets before approval

C) UNIQUE EU BURDEN - GMO Requirement:
   FDA: No requirement
   EMA: ALL in vivo gene therapies must obtain separate GMO (Genetically Modified
        Organism) regulatory approval BEFORE initiating any clinical trial
   Impact: Adds 6-12 months and additional regulatory burden to EU development

D) Post-Approval Reimbursement Barrier (EU ONLY):
   FDA: Medicare/commercial reimbursement negotiated separately
   EMA: Post-EMA approval, companies must negotiate HTA (Health Technology Assessment)
        with EACH Member State individually
   Impact: Adds 1-3 years; has caused approved ATMPs to be withdrawn from EU market
   Example: Alofisel (darvadstrocel) approved by EMA March 2018, withdrawn from EU
            market December 2024 due to reimbursement challenges

QUESTION 3: Hospital exemption rules — do they create a path for aging therapies?
================================================================================

FINDING: Theoretically possible but severely limited in practice for aging applications

Article 28 Hospital Exemption Framework:

Legal Basis:
- Article 28(2) of ATMP Regulation 1394/2007
- Article 3(7) of Directive 2001/83/EC

Permitted Use:
1. Non-routine basis (custom-made, not mass-manufactured)
2. Prepared on patient-specific basis
3. Used within same EU Member State
4. Hospital setting only
5. Under exclusive professional responsibility of medical practitioner
6. Individual medical prescription for individual patient

Quality Requirements:
- Must comply with GMP standards equivalent to authorized ATMPs
- Specific national quality/traceability standards
- Pharmacovigilance and adverse event reporting mandatory

Limitations for Aging-Focused ATMPs:

1) NO REGULATORY GUIDANCE ON AGING:
   - EMA has NOT issued specific guidance on what qualifies as "aging therapy"
     under Article 28
   - Each Member State interprets eligibility criteria differently
   - Significant implementation variability across EU (Belgium, Spain, France,
     Netherlands, Sweden, Germany all have different policies)

2) INDIVIDUAL PATIENT REQUIREMENT:
   - HE is designed for custom, patient-specific therapies
   - NOT suitable for standardized, off-the-shelf longevity products
   - Cannot be used as commercial pathway for aging supplement-like products

3) CLINICAL TRIAL PREFERENCE:
   - Where investigational therapy is available in authorized clinical trial,
     patients are NOT eligible for HE
   - Preference given to trials (higher safety standards)
   - This effectively blocks HE for aging-targeted therapies likely to be in
     concurrent clinical development

4) PHARMACOVIGILANCE BURDEN:
   - Post-use monitoring and adverse event reporting required
   - Must comply with national traceability requirements
   - Adds administrative complexity

Member State Variability Example:
- Belgium: Relatively permissive HE framework; active use for experimental cell therapies
- Germany: More restrictive; prefers clinical trial pathway
- France: Mid-range approach with specific quality standards
- Spain: Has established guidelines (TERAV network) but still limited aging-specific pathways

VERDICT:
HE does NOT provide a practical fast-track for aging-focused ATMP development due to:
- Lack of explicit regulatory guidance on aging indication
- Individual patient requirement (not suitable for standardized products)
- Clinical trial preference (blocks HE eligibility when trials exist)
- Member State variability and uncertainty
- Heavy post-use pharmacovigilance burden

Clinical trials remain the preferred regulatory pathway for aging-targeted ATMPs.

QUESTION 4: EMA stance on aging as a therapeutic indication?
================================================================================

FINDING: AGING IS NOT RECOGNIZED as valid therapeutic indication by EMA or FDA

Explicit Regulatory Position:

Statement: "Regulatory bodies around the world, such as the FDA or EMA, do not yet
recognize geroscience-guided clinical outcomes as a path to regulatory approval."
Source: PMC9009114 (2022)

Specific Barriers Identified:

1) NO REGULATORY FRAMEWORK FOR "GEROTHERAPEUTICS":
   - Neither FDA nor EMA has established formal regulatory pathway for aging-targeted
     therapeutics
   - Scoping review found complete absence of government-established frameworks for
     this therapeutic class
   - Existing frameworks (ATMP, NDA, BLA) designed for disease targets, not aging process

2) AGING BIOMARKERS NOT QUALIFIED:
   - Biomarkers: Cellular senescence markers, telomere length, epigenetic clocks,
     NAD+ levels, etc.
   - Status: NOT qualified by FDA or EMA as surrogate endpoints
   - Reason: Insufficient longitudinal validation studies linking biomarkers to
     clinical outcomes
   - Impact: Cannot use aging biomarkers to support clinical trial endpoints without
     full validation

3) NO CONSENSUS ON CLINICAL ENDPOINTS:
   - Specific age-related diseases have defined endpoints:
     * Alzheimer's disease: ADAS-cog, CDR scores
     * Osteoarthritis: WOMAC, pain scales
     * Frailty: Frailty Index (specific measures)
   - Aging as process: NO agreed-upon clinical trial endpoints
   - "Healthspan" vs "lifespan" extensions: undefned in regulatory context

4) EMA'S CURRENT GERIATRIC STRATEGY:
   - EMA guidance exists for medicines for OLDER PEOPLE
   - Focus: disease management in elderly (polypharmacy, cognitive decline, frailty)
   - NOT on aging itself as therapeutic target
   - Guidance documents address age-related conditions, not aging process

Workaround Strategy:
Companies seeking marketing authorization for aging-related therapies frame as:
- "Age-related macular degeneration" (specific disease)
- "Alzheimer's disease" (age-associated but defined disease)
- "Osteoarthritis" (degenerative disease, common in aging)
Rather than "aging," "anti-aging," or "longevity"

Geroscience Research Challenges Identified:

From recent 2025-2026 literature:
1) Unclear clinical trial design standards for aging interventions
2) Lack of validated biomarkers for regulatory endpoints
3) Insufficient evidence linking interventions to lifespan extension
4) No data on healthspan improvement in aging-targeted therapies
5) Economic constraints on regulatory agency engagement
6) Lack of policy-level commitment to develop aging-specific frameworks

EMA's Current Position (2025):
- Aging remains outside formal regulatory purview
- Focus on treating age-related diseases with defined endpoints
- No announced plans to develop aging-specific ATMP pathway
- Hospital exemption guidance does not address aging therapies specifically

QUESTION 5: How does EU handle longevity supplement claims vs. US DSHEA?
================================================================================

FINDING: Fundamental regulatory divergence. EU is highly restrictive; US is permissive.

EU FRAMEWORK: EFSA Health Claims Authorization Required

Regulatory Basis:
- Regulation (EC) No 1924/2006 on nutrition and health claims
- European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) enforces through Scientific Panel on Dietetic Products

How It Works:
1) Company submits dossier to EFSA with health claim + scientific evidence
2) EFSA conducts independent scientific evaluation (typically 12-24 months)
3) EFSA recommends authorization or rejection
4) European Commission makes final decision
5) Claim can be used ONLY if on approved list

Aging/Longevity Claims Status in EU:

EXPLICITLY NOT AUTHORIZED:
- "Healthy aging" - Rejected as too vague/physiological
- "Protection of cells from premature ageing" - Rejected as non-specific
- "Cellular repair" - Rejected as implying mechanism not proven
- "Anti-aging" - Not approved
- "Promotes longevity" - Not approved
- "Extends lifespan" - Not approved
- "Slows aging" - Not approved

Current Status of Popular Longevity Ingredients:
- NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide): NO authorized health claims; EFSA rejected claims
  for immune support, DNA/lipid protection, cardiovascular health (insufficient evidence)
- Quercetin: NO aging-related claims authorized
- Resveratrol: Limited authorized claims; no aging indication
- NAD+ boosters: NO aging-specific authorizations

How Companies Comply:
Option 1: Seek EFSA authorization (lengthy, difficult)
  - Most aging-related claims rejected
  - Successful authorizations rare
  - Cost: €50,000-500,000
  - Timeline: 18-24+ months

Option 2: Use only generic structure claims unrelated to aging
  - Example: "Supports normal immune function" (if authorized)
  - Cannot claim aging benefit
  - Severely limits market differentiation

Option 3: Face regulatory enforcement
  - National Competent Authorities can take action
  - Penalties: Product withdrawal, fines, criminal liability for false claims

Regulatory Enforcement:
- EFSA maintains "List of health claims" (regularly updated)
- National regulators actively monitor supplement labeling
- Violations result in product removal from EU market
- Cross-border enforcement through EU mechanisms

---

US FRAMEWORK: DSHEA Permissive Structure/Function Claims

Regulatory Basis:
- Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA), 1994
- FDA oversees but does NOT pre-approve structure/function claims

How It Works:
1) Company formulates supplement
2) Company makes internal decision on claim(s)
3) Company notifies FDA (not FDA approval, just notification)
4) Product can be sold 30-90 days later with NO pre-market clearance
5) FDA can only challenge if claim is false/misleading AFTER product on market

Aging/Longevity Claims Permitted in US:

EXPLICITLY ALLOWED:
- "Supports healthy aging"
- "Promotes longevity"
- "Anti-aging"
- "Supports cognitive health as you age"
- "Supports joint health in aging"
- "Helps maintain energy levels with age"
- "Supports healthy skin as you age"

Legal Standard for Claims:
- Must be "truthful and not misleading"
- Must not imply drug efficacy (cannot claim cures/treats/prevents disease)
- Must be structure/function claim (describes physiological effect)
- Efficacy proof NOT REQUIRED by law (only substantiation to defend if challenged)

Burden of Proof:
- FDA must prove claim false (not company must prove true)
- If FDA challenges, burden shifts to company to show substantiation exists
- Substantiation standard: "competent and reliable scientific evidence"
- What counts: Peer-reviewed studies, expert opinion, reasonable interpretation of data

Market Access:
- 30-90 day notification period
- Product can go to market immediately
- Minimal regulatory gatekeeping
- Can modify claims without new notification (if change is minor)

Enforcement:
- FDA can only act if claims are demonstrably false
- Private litigation also possible (false advertising)
- Penalties: FTC/FDA action, product relabeling, in rare cases criminal liability
- BUT: FDA has limited resources; many products with questionable claims remain on market

Examples of US Market:

Products with Longevity Claims Currently Sold:
- NMN supplements: "Supports healthy aging," "promotes cellular energy"
- Resveratrol products: "Anti-aging support," "supports longevity"
- Senolytics (fisetin-based): "Targets cellular senescence," "supports healthy aging"
- NAD+ boosters: Multiple longevity claims on labels

Can Companies Do This?
YES. Without EFSA authorization requirement, US market for longevity supplements is
vastly larger and more accessible than EU.

---

COMPARATIVE TABLE:

                        EU (EFSA)                    US (DSHEA)
Pre-market auth         REQUIRED                     NOT REQUIRED
Aging claims allowed    NO (mostly prohibited)       YES (freely allowed)
Efficacy proof needed   YES (before approval)        NO (optional substantiation)
Review timeline         18-24+ months                30-90 days notification
Claim authorization    Pre-market EFSA list         Company decides (post-market review)
Burden of proof         Company proves claim true    FDA must prove claim false
Market complexity       High (per-country review)    Streamlined (notification only)
Cost to authorize claim €50k-500k + 2 years         Minimal (internal substantiation)
Enforcement            Active (routine monitoring)   Reactive (FDA challenges claims)
Products on market      Very limited aging claims    Many longevity products available

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS FOR LONGEVITY BUSINESSES:

EU Strategy:
- Avoid explicit aging claims → regulatory nightmare
- Use generic claims only if authorized
- Clinical trial/medical device pathway more viable than supplement
- Market focus: Specific age-related diseases (Alzheimer's, osteoarthritis)
- Geographic restriction: Cannot use US-style marketing in EU

US Strategy:
- Longevity supplement market is accessible with minimal gatekeeping
- Can market "supports healthy aging," "anti-aging" without EFSA
- Maintain internal substantiation (publications, reasonable interpretation)
- Can leverage geroscience research to defend claims if FDA challenges
- Global arbitrage: US market 5-10x larger for longevity supplements than EU

SUMMARY: KEY REGULATORY GAPS FOR AGING/LONGEVITY THERAPIES
================================================================================

1. NO APPROVED ATMP FOR AGING
   - 24+ ATMPs approved in EU, ZERO with aging indication
   - Regulatory pathway does not exist for aging-targeted therapies
   - Hospital exemption not designed/authorized for aging applications

2. FDA AND EMA BOTH LACK AGING FRAMEWORK
   - Neither regulator recognizes aging as valid therapeutic target
   - Aging biomarkers not qualified for regulatory use
   - No consensus on clinical trial endpoints for "longevity"
   - Workaround: Frame as specific disease (Alzheimer's, osteoarthritis, etc.)

3. SUPPLEMENT MARKET: STARK EU-US DIVIDE
   - EU: Aging claims prohibited without EFSA authorization (nearly impossible)
   - US: Aging claims freely allowed with minimal pre-market review
   - Creates regulatory arbitrage favoring US market development

4. REGULATORY PATHWAY OPTIONS FOR AGING THERAPIES:
   Option A: Target specific age-related disease (FDA/EMA approved pathway)
   Option B: Develop in US using DSHEA supplement pathway (faster)
   Option C: Hospital exemption (EU only; limited applicability)
   Option D: Clinical trial in geroscience endpoints (emerging; not established)
   Option E: Await regulatory reform (European Commission reviewing, timeline unclear)

5. EMERGING REGULATORY MOVEMENT:
   - European Commission studying HE reform (started late 2023)
   - Proposal for revision of EU pharma legislation under discussion
   - No announced aging-specific framework yet
   - EMA academic/non-profit support pilot may help smaller developers
