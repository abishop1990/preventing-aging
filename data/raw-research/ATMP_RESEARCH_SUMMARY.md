# EU ATMP Regulatory Framework for Aging Therapies: Executive Summary

## Quick Findings

### 1. How does the EU ATMP framework differ from FDA for gene/cell therapies?

**FDA is faster and more flexible:**
- FDA offers RMAT (Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy) designation and other expedited pathways allowing earlier approval based on surrogate endpoints and real-world evidence
- EMA requires larger clinical datasets, longer monitoring periods, and full centralized review (CAT + CHMP committees)
- **GMO requirement unique to EU**: All in vivo gene therapies must obtain separate Genetically Modified Organism approval before any clinical trial in Europe—no equivalent FDA requirement
- **Post-approval reimbursement barrier in EU**: Even after EMA approval, companies must negotiate HTA (health technology assessment) with each Member State separately. This has caused approved ATMPs (e.g., Alofisel) to be withdrawn from EU market
- **Timeline impact**: EMA approvals typically longer; reimbursement negotiations add 1-3 additional years

### 2. What ATMPs have been approved in Europe? Any relevant to aging?

**24+ ATMPs approved as of May 2025, including:**
- CAR-T therapies: Yescarta, Kymriah, Abecma, Breyanzi, Carvykti, Tecartus (all oncology)
- Gene therapies: Luxturna (retinal dystrophy), Zynteglo (beta-thalassemia), Zolgensma (SMA), Roctavian (hemophilia A), Skysona, Libmeldy, Upstaza
- Tissue-engineered: Alofisel (Crohn's fistulas—withdrawn Dec 2024), Strimvelis (SCID)

**Aging-specific ATMPs: NONE.** No currently approved ATMP lists aging, longevity, or senescence as indication. Tissue-engineered products target specific tissue repair, not aging per se.

### 3. Hospital exemption rules — do they create a path for aging therapies?

**Theoretically yes, practically limited:**

Article 28 of ATMP Regulation 1394/2007 allows non-routine, patient-specific ATMPs to be used in hospitals without marketing authorization if:
- Prepared on custom basis (not routine/off-the-shelf)
- Used within same Member State in hospital
- Under physician's individual prescription
- Proper pharmacovigilance/traceability maintained

**BUT critical limitations for aging therapies:**
- NO regulatory guidance on what qualifies as valid "aging therapy" for hospital exemption
- Implementation varies dramatically by Member State (some permissive, others very restrictive)
- Clinical trials preferred over HE when available—patients ineligible for HE if trial participation possible
- Designed for patient-specific therapies, not standardized longevity products
- Heavy pharmacovigilance burden (post-use monitoring required)

**Verdict**: HE is narrow pathway, not a fast-track for aging-focused ATMP development.

### 4. EMA stance on aging as an indication?

**Clear regulatory position: Aging is NOT recognized as a valid therapeutic indication.**

**Key barriers:**
- No regulatory pathway exists for "gerotherapeutics" or aging as disease target (FDA or EMA)
- Aging biomarkers (senescence markers, epigenetic clocks, telomere length) are NOT qualified by EMA/FDA as surrogate endpoints—insufficient longitudinal validation linking them to clinical outcomes
- No consensus on clinical trial endpoints for "aging" as target
- EMA has geriatric guidance for specific age-related diseases (Alzheimer's, osteoarthritis) but NOT for aging itself

**Workaround**: Companies frame products for specific diseases common in aging (age-related macular degeneration, Alzheimer's) rather than "aging"

**Recent research gap**: Geroscience field identifies major challenge—regulators lack frameworks for true longevity/anti-aging interventions; no clear path to demonstrate lifespan or healthspan extension

### 5. How does EU handle longevity supplement claims vs. US DSHEA?

**EU: Highly restrictive. Pre-market approval required for health claims.**

EFSA (European Food Safety Authority) Health Claims Regulation 1924/2006:
- All claims must be pre-authorized by EFSA before marketing
- Aging/longevity claims are **largely PROHIBITED**: "healthy aging," "cellular repair," "anti-aging," "promotes longevity" are NOT approved
- Recent products (NMN, quercetin) FAILED authorization—insufficient evidence
- No "structure/function" claim loophole like US
- Claims take 18-24+ months to authorize; most aging-related claims rejected

**US: Very permissive. Minimal pre-market review for structure/function claims.**

DSHEA (Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act, 1994):
- NO pre-market FDA approval needed for structure/function claims
- Can market: "supports healthy aging," "promotes longevity," "supports joint health" WITHOUT proving efficacy
- Only requirement: claims must be truthful and non-misleading; substantiation doesn't need to be pre-approved
- Products can launch 30-90 days after FDA notification
- Burden of proof on FDA to challenge (not on manufacturer to pre-prove efficacy)

**Practical difference:**
- EU: Longevity supplement company = needs EFSA approval (very hard for aging claims) or can only use generic health claims unrelated to aging
- US: Longevity supplement company = can freely market aging-related claims with minimal gatekeeping, provided internal substantiation exists

### Bottom Line for Aging/Longevity Therapies in EU vs. US:

| Aspect | EU | US |
|--------|----|----|
| **ATMPs for aging** | Not recognized; no approved aging-indication ATMPs | Same regulatory gap, but FDA pathways faster |
| **Hospital exemption pathway** | Very limited; designed for patient-specific, not aging products | N/A (US has no exact equivalent) |
| **Aging biomarkers** | Not qualified; no regulatory pathway | Same—biomarkers not qualified |
| **Supplement longevity claims** | PROHIBITED unless pre-authorized by EFSA (very rare) | Broadly ALLOWED under DSHEA with minimal review |
| **Market access speed** | Slow (18-24+ months for claims authorization) | Fast (30-90 days notification) |
| **Reimbursement** | Post-approval HTA negotiations add 1-3 years | N/A—varies by payer/insurance |
