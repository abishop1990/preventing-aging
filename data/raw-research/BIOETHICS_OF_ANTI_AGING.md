# Bioethics of Anti-Aging Interventions and Life Extension

**Last verified: March 2026 | Sources: PubMed systematic search, confirmed citations only**

This document synthesizes the peer-reviewed bioethics literature on anti-aging interventions, life extension, and the treatment vs. enhancement distinction. Every citation below was confirmed against live PubMed records. Where PubMed does not index a source, that is noted and the claim is documented as established consensus rather than direct search result.

---

## 1. Treatment vs. Enhancement: Where Is the Ethical Line?

### The Core Problem

The treatment/enhancement distinction asks: is slowing aging a medical treatment (restoring normal function) or an enhancement (exceeding normal function)? The answer has profound consequences for insurance coverage, regulatory approval, physician duties, and the moral permissibility of intervention.

The distinction is under serious attack. Traditional medicine treats disease; it does not treat aging. But if aging itself is a disease — or a root cause of all chronic disease — then treating it becomes treatment, not enhancement.

### Key Papers

**Schweda M & Marckmann G (2013).** "How do we want to grow old? Anti-ageing-medicine and the scope of public healthcare in liberal democracies." *Bioethics* 27(7):357-64. **PMID: 22500556**
- Argues that anti-aging medicine sits in a gray zone: public healthcare systems do not cover it, leaving access to market forces.
- Proposes a procedural (deliberative-democratic) framework, rather than an essentialist ("what is natural") or transcendental framework, for determining what belongs in public healthcare.
- Key insight: The question is not "is aging natural?" but "what do we collectively decide is a legitimate healthcare priority?"

**McKiddy S (2025).** "Should Clinicians Be Agents of Anti-Aging?" *AMA Journal of Ethics* 27(12):E859-865. **PMID: 41411401**
- Directly addresses whether clinicians should prescribe gerotherapeutics (candidate anti-aging drugs from geroscience) and how this should be integrated ethically into practice.
- Argues that the line must be drawn at health equity: geroscience's impact depends on whether its benefits are aligned with individual values, health equity across populations, and prevention of age-related conditions. Without equity alignment, even legitimate treatment becomes a vector for widening health disparities.
- Recommends systems-level integration criteria before clinical adoption.

**Masny M (2023).** "Healthspan extension, completeness of life and justice." *Bioethics* 37(3):239-245. **PMID: 36525613**
- Princeton philosopher. Argues from a justice standpoint that anti-aging technology should be treated as treatment, not enhancement.
- Core argument: Justice requires that each person have sufficient opportunities for a "complete life." Many people currently lack these opportunities due to premature age-related decline. Therefore, making anti-aging technology unconditionally available is not enhancement — it is fulfilling a basic justice obligation.
- If adopted, this argument collapses the treatment/enhancement distinction for aging: slowing aging becomes as obligatory as treating cancer.

**Ehni HJ & Marckmann G (2008).** "The normative dimensions of extending the human lifespan by age-related biomedical innovations." *Rejuvenation Res* 11(5):965-9. **PMID: 18922049**
- Early systematic attempt to build a normative framework for aging interventions.
- Argues that three dimensions are required: (1) a theory of the good life (what counts as benefit), (2) deontological constraints on interventions, and (3) a distributive justice model for access.
- The framework requires all three simultaneously — consequentialist reasoning alone is insufficient.

### The Regulatory Gap

Under current FDA definitions, aging is not a disease, so drugs cannot receive approval for "treating aging." This is why the TAME trial (metformin for aging) is being run with composite disease endpoints (cardiovascular disease, cancer, dementia) as its primary outcomes rather than aging itself. The treatment/enhancement line is therefore currently drawn at a regulatory level: anything targeting aging is by default an "enhancement" and therefore cannot be the primary endpoint of a drug approval trial. Khaltourina et al. (2020) — see Section 2 below — directly challenged this by arguing that aging meets ICD-11 disease criteria, which would have regulatory downstream consequences.

## 2. WHO / ICD-11 Classification of Aging: What Actually Happened

### The Key Paper

**Khaltourina D, Matveyev Y, Alekseev A, Cortese F, Iovița A (2020).** "Aging Fits the Disease Criteria of the International Classification of Diseases." *Mechanisms of Ageing and Development* 189:111230. **PMID: 32251691**

This is the most important paper on the ICD-11/aging classification question. Full abstract details:

- Applied WHO's own ICD-11 disease criteria systematically to human biological aging.
- Criteria include: diagnostics, mechanisms, course and outcomes, known interventions, and linkage to genetic/environmental factors.
- Conclusion: Aging meets all ICD-11 disease criteria. It can be diagnosed (frailty indices, blood-based biomarkers), has known causal mechanisms (senescence, proteostasis failure, mitochondrial decline, etc.), has a predictable course, and is influenced by known genetic and environmental factors.
- **Critical outcome:** The authors submitted their proposal to the ICD-11 Joint Task Force. This led directly to the inclusion of the extension code **XT9T ("Ageing-related")** in the "Causality" section of ICD-11, which took effect in 2022.

### What the ICD-11 Code Means — and What It Doesn't

The XT9T code is an **extension code**, not a primary disease code. This is important:
- Extension codes in ICD-11 modify the interpretation of a primary code; they cannot stand alone.
- In practice, XT9T allows a physician to code a condition (e.g., cardiovascular disease, dementia) as "ageing-related," enabling epidemiological tracking of the aging contribution.
- It does **not** classify aging itself as a primary disease. This was a deliberate compromise.

The Latin American Academy of Medicine for Older Persons took note. **Cano-Gutierrez et al. (2021)** published a position paper in *Rev Panam Salud Publica* (PMID: 34413882) examining the implications of ICD-11 for older adults, including the new "old age" coding provisions. They raised concerns that classifying aging as a disease could medicalize normal aging, lead to inappropriate treatment-seeking, and increase ageist assumptions in clinical care.

### The "Ageing-related" Code vs. a "Disease of Aging" Code

There is ongoing debate about whether a future ICD revision should go further and create a primary disease code for biological aging (analogous to how HIV was eventually coded as a disease rather than just a risk factor). Supporters argue this would unlock regulatory pathways for anti-aging drug development. Opponents (including the Latin American geriatrics academy) argue it would pathologize normal life stages and distort clinical priorities.

The WHO has not committed to going further than XT9T. The 2022 ICD-11 implementation represents the current settled position.

## 3. Equity and Access: Who Gets Anti-Aging?

### The Central Problem

If effective anti-aging interventions are developed, wealthy individuals and wealthy nations will access them first — as has happened with every other medical breakthrough. Ethicists have identified this as potentially the most serious objection to anti-aging research: not that it won't work, but that it will create a two-tier mortality system, with rich people living substantially longer than poor people, compounding every existing inequality across compounded decades.

### Key Papers

**Farrelly C (2022/2024).** "Aging, Equality and the Human Healthspan." *HEC Forum* 36(2):187-205. **PMID: 36348214** (Published online 2022; print 2024. Free PMC: PMC9644010)
- Reviews John Davis's *New Methuselahs* (MIT Press, 2018) framework which divides populations into "Haves," "Have-nots," and "Will-nots" regarding life extension technology.
- Farrelly's critique: Davis focuses too much on futuristic radical life extension and not enough on near-term healthspan inequality. The "aging status quo" is already deeply unjust — socioeconomic status is a profound predictor of healthy life expectancy, and wealthy people already live significantly longer in good health.
- Counter-argument: Framing anti-aging as an equity issue means current health inequalities are already an emergency. An anti-aging intervention that benefits all is not the problem; it's the solution. The real equity demand is for universal access, not for halting development.
- Conclusion: An intervention that slows molecular and cellular aging "will likely be one of the most important public health advancements of the twenty-first century" precisely because aging is the universal risk factor — reducing it would be egalitarian in effect if access is guaranteed.

**Masny M (2023) (see above, PMID: 36525613).**
- Makes the strongest pro-universal-access argument in the literature: justice requires that anti-aging technology be made unconditionally available to everyone, because the "complete life" standard requires it. The argument is explicitly not consequentialist — it's a deontological justice claim.

**Ehni HJ & Marckmann G (2008) (see above, PMID: 18922049).**
- Specifically frames the access question within theories of distributive justice: Rawlsian (maximizing the position of the worst-off), egalitarian (equal access), and sufficiency-based (everyone gets enough). Argues no single theory fully resolves the access problem and that a multi-level framework is required.

**Schweda M & Marckmann G (2013) (see above, PMID: 22500556).**
- Key framing: Anti-aging medicine currently sits outside public healthcare in liberal democracies, meaning access is determined entirely by market forces. This is the current default — not a chosen policy position, but an absence of policy. The question "who should pay for this?" has not been democratically deliberated.

### The "Longevity Divide" Concern

The socioeconomic gradient in life expectancy already exists: in many wealthy nations, the difference in healthy life expectancy between the wealthiest and poorest quintiles exceeds a decade. If anti-aging interventions compound this by extending healthy life by 10–20 additional years for those who can afford it, the divide becomes a chasm. Ethicists use this not to oppose anti-aging research but to argue for proactive policy frameworks now, before effective treatments exist.

## 4. Population-Level Concerns: Overpopulation, Resource Allocation, Pensions

### Overpopulation

**Wareham C (2015).** "Slowed ageing, welfare, and population problems." *Theoretical Medicine and Bioethics* 36(5):321-40. **PMID: 26246312**
- The most thorough philosophical treatment of the overpopulation objection specifically for slowed-aging interventions (as distinct from immortality or radical life extension).
- Examines two problems: (1) overpopulation (too many people alive simultaneously, reducing average welfare), and (2) underpopulation (life extension reduces births, so fewer total people exist across time, reducing total welfare).
- Conclusion on overpopulation: "Highly unlikely to result from technologies that slow ageing." Key reason: slowed aging delays death but does not remove the eventual mortality ceiling. If aging is merely slowed (not stopped), total lifespan increases modestly. The demographic impact is comparable to what already happened with 20th-century lifespan increases, which did not produce the predicted population collapse.
- Conclusion on underpopulation: The underpopulation problem rests on claims about life extension (e.g., that people would stop having children entirely) that are false for slowed-aging technologies specifically.

**Cutas DE (2008).** "Life extension, overpopulation and the right to life: against lethal ethics." *Journal of Medical Ethics* 34(9):e7. **PMID: 18757626**
- More confrontational position: Even if overpopulation were a real risk, arguments from overpopulation cannot ethically demand halting research or restricting access. The right to life cannot be extinguished by demographic calculation.
- Argues that "lethal ethics" — positions that justify allowing people to die sooner in order to solve population problems — are morally incoherent.

**Han ZZ & de Magalhães JP (2026).** "The ethics case for longevity science." *Ageing Research Reviews* 117:103054. **PMID: 41654143**
- Most recent comprehensive review of the ethics debate, from a pro-longevity perspective (de Magalhães has industry conflicts of interest as CSO of YouthBio Therapeutics, disclosed).
- Argues that ethical debate has been "dominated by consequentialist framings, balancing potential benefits against fears of overpopulation, inequality, or loss of meaning."
- Grounds the pro-longevity case in autonomy, self-ownership, and intrinsic value of life — not just outcomes.
- Addresses three categories of objections: (1) "naturalness" — the naturalistic fallacy, rebuttable on standard philosophical grounds; (2) societal concerns about resources, justice, stagnation — addressed by arguing the overpopulation concern is empirically weak and the justice concern demands universal access rather than rejection of research; (3) individual worries about meaning and boredom — addressed with the argument that humans have shown capacity to find meaning in longer lives.
- Strongest claim: "The burden now falls on defenders of forced ageing to explain why preventable suffering should continue."

### Pensions and Retirement Systems

The peer-reviewed bioethics literature is sparse on specific pension analysis (this is primarily an economics and policy science literature rather than a bioethics literature). The core concern — that pension and retirement systems are actuarially calibrated to lifespan expectations, and that significant lifespan extension would bankrupt these systems — is widely noted in the background of ethics papers but rarely analyzed in depth in PubMed-indexed bioethics journals.

The implicit framing in papers like Wareham (2015) is that this is a solvable policy problem: retirement ages would rise, and intergenerational resource distribution would be renegotiated. This is treated as a logistical challenge rather than a decisive ethical objection to life extension research.

## 5. Informed Consent and Self-Experimentation: Bryan Johnson and Biohackers

### The Consent Problem

The peer-reviewed literature on consent and self-experimentation specifically in the longevity/biohacking context is thin in PubMed. However, two important papers address adjacent issues:

**Ienca M & Vayena E (2020).** "'Hunting Down My Son's Killer': New Roles of Patients in Treatment Discovery and Ethical Uncertainty." *Journal of Bioethics Inquiry* 17(1):37-47. **PMID: 32103412**
- Examines cases where desperate patients and families undertake self-directed medical experimentation outside clinical trial structures, raising questions about the ethics of patient-driven research.
- Key tensions identified: (1) autonomy vs. protection from harm; (2) individual experimentation vs. collective scientific knowledge (n=1 experiments are scientifically uninformative); (3) the risk that high-profile self-experimenters normalize unsafe practices for others who are less informed.

**Guerrini CJ et al. (2022).** "Core values of genomic citizen science." *BioSocieties* 17(2):203-228. **PMID: 36032504**
- Studies citizen scientists engaged in self-experimentation in biological domains including genomics and biohacking.
- Identifies key values citizen scientists invoke: autonomy, democratization of science, anti-paternalism. These values are in direct tension with IRB-based research ethics frameworks built around protection from harm.

### The Bryan Johnson Problem Specifically

Bryan Johnson (the Protocol longevity self-experimenter) does not appear in PubMed-indexed literature by name. However, his approach raises issues that the academic literature does address:

1. **N=1 is scientifically uninformative.** Multiple interventions simultaneously in a single individual cannot be separated. Protocol-style self-experimentation generates publicity, not science. This is distinct from, and potentially harmful to, legitimate longevity research by creating a false sense of progress and potentially prompting others to adopt risky protocols.

2. **Wealth-based access to experimental protocols.** Johnson's protocol reportedly costs millions of dollars annually. This exemplifies the equity concern: extreme self-experimentation is only available to the ultra-wealthy, raising questions about whether results (if any) would generalize to normal-access populations.

3. **Conflict of interest in reporting.** Johnson has financial interests in several of the products and methods he uses, creating serious bias concerns in how results are reported publicly.

4. **Regulatory gray zones.** Many interventions in biohacker protocols (off-label rapamycin, experimental senolytics, gene therapies administered abroad) operate in regulatory gray zones. The ethics literature on "medical tourism for enhancement" (accessing unproven treatments in jurisdictions with less oversight) is directly relevant.

5. **The Belmont Report's applicability.** The foundational framework for research ethics (Belmont Report, 1979) requires independent ethics review, informed consent with full disclosure of risks, and justice in subject selection. Self-experimentation outside any IRB process circumvents all three. The key question is whether Belmont principles apply to self-research at all — traditional interpretation is that they apply to research "involving human subjects" as subjects of another researcher's inquiry, not self-directed research. This creates a significant ethical lacuna.

## 6. The "Duty to Age" vs. "Duty to Prevent Suffering" Debate

### The Pro-Aging ("Bioconservative") Position

The strongest philosophical case for accepting aging comes from Leon Kass (2002, "L'Chaim and Its Limits: Why Not Immortality?" *First Things*) and Francis Fukuyama (2002, *Our Posthuman Future: Consequences of the Biotechnology Revolution*). Neither is indexed in PubMed, but their arguments are extensively discussed in the indexed literature.

**Kass's Core Argument (not PubMed-indexed):** Mortality gives life its urgency, seriousness, and meaning. Immortal beings would lack the scarcity of time that makes choices significant. Aging and death are part of what it means to be genuinely human. The "wisdom of repugnance" — the visceral reaction against radical life extension — reflects a deep moral intuition deserving respect. Death is not merely a problem to be solved; it is constitutive of human dignity.

**Fukuyama's Argument (not PubMed-indexed):** Human nature — including our mortality — is the ground of our dignity and the basis of human rights. Biotechnological transformation of human nature, including radical life extension, threatens the common humanity that grounds equal rights. There is a "Factor X" — an essence of human nature — that demands protection from biotechnological alteration.

These arguments appear in the PubMed-indexed literature primarily as positions to be rebutted:

**Han ZZ & de Magalhães (2026, PMID: 41654143)** explicitly address the "naturalness" objection:
- The naturalistic fallacy: "natural" does not mean "good." Smallpox, cancer, and infant mortality are all natural; we do not therefore tolerate them.
- The "loss of meaning" objection requires showing that meaning is logically dependent on mortality — a claim that is not established. Humans in longer-lived societies (Japan, Scandinavia) have not reported meaninglessness as a consequence of longer life expectancy.

**Wareham CS (2021).** "Between hoping to die and longing to live longer." *History and Philosophy of the Life Sciences* 43(2):40. **PMID: 33754219**
- Makes a distinction overlooked in the ethics literature: between *hoping* to die (welcoming death when it comes, given a full life) and *longing* not to have been born mortal. These are different psychological relationships to mortality, and conflating them distorts the ethical analysis.
- There are genuine human psychology-based reasons to be cautious about *how much* one aims to live — without this constituting a "duty to age."

**Wareham CS (2020).** "Genome Editing for Longer Lives: The Problem of Loneliness." *Journal of Bioethics Inquiry* 17(2):309-314. **PMID: 32152896**
- Identifies a practical concern that the "duty to age" literature conflates with the philosophical case: if you live dramatically longer than your social network (friends, partners, family), you face profound loneliness. This is not a metaphysical point about human nature — it's an empirical wellbeing concern. The response is not to accept aging but to address the social infrastructure for long lives.

### The Pro-Prevention Position

The strongest counter to Kass/Fukuyama in the indexed literature is in **Han & de Magalhães (2026)**:
- "The burden now falls on defenders of forced ageing to explain why preventable suffering should continue."
- Aging causes enormous suffering — dementia, cancer, cardiovascular disease, frailty. If we can prevent this suffering, we have a strong positive duty to do so, not merely a permission.
- The autonomy argument: individuals should be free to choose how long to live. A "duty to age" is paternalistic — it imposes a particular view of the good life (one that includes death at a normal age) on individuals who may not share it.

**Cutas DE (2008, PMID: 18757626)** makes the rights-based version: the right to life is not extinguished by appeals to naturalness, social utility, or philosophical notions about meaning. No argument about what death contributes to human existence can justify actively preventing people from seeking to live longer.

## 7. Leon Kass / Francis Fukuyama Arguments vs. Transhumanism

### The Debate Structure

This is primarily a book-length and journal-article debate that predates much of the current geroscience. Key positions:

**Bioconservative position (Kass, Fukuyama, Michael Sandel):**
- Human enhancement, including life extension, threatens human dignity by erasing the natural givenness of human life.
- The "giftedness" of life — its unchosen character — grounds an appropriate humility and solidarity.
- "Hyperparenting" analogies: engineering children's traits, like engineering one's own longevity, represents a failure to accept the limitations that constitute genuine love and community.

**Transhumanist position (Nick Bostrom, Julian Savulescu, John Harris):**
- The "yuck factor" (Kass's "wisdom of repugnance") is historically unreliable as a moral guide. It was invoked against interracial marriage, organ transplantation, and in vitro fertilization — all now accepted.
- There is a positive duty to enhance when enhancement reduces suffering. Calling radical life extension "unnatural" is a category error.
- Bostrom (2005, *Journal of Medical Ethics*): "The Fable of the Dragon-Tyrant" — aging is the dragon, and our acceptance of it is not wisdom but learned helplessness in the face of a solvable problem.

In the PubMed literature, the transhumanist position has largely prevailed among bioethicists who engage directly with geroscience, while the bioconservative position persists among philosophers of medicine and medical humanists.

**Roduit JAR, Eichinger T & Glannon W (2018).** "Science fiction and human enhancement: radical life-extension in the movie 'In Time' (2011)." *Medicine, Health Care and Philosophy* 21(3):287-293. **PMID: 29560602**
- Uses the sci-fi scenario of *In Time* (a world where currency is measured in years of life) to explore what radical life extension would do to social structures. The film depicts extreme inequality of lifespan by wealth — a caricature that nonetheless maps onto real equity concerns.
- Useful for illustrating how equity arguments and lifespan extension arguments interact.

**Blackford R (2009).** "Moral pluralism versus the total view: why Singer is wrong about radical life extension." *Journal of Medical Ethics* 35(12):747-52. **PMID: 19948931**
- Peter Singer argued from utilitarian total-view population ethics that radical life extension could be wrong because it crowds out the creation of new, potentially happier people.
- Blackford argues that moral pluralism (the view that individual rights to life and autonomy cannot be traded off against aggregate population welfare) defeats Singer's conclusion.
- Relevant to understanding the population-ethics dimension of the debate.

## 8. Animal Research Ethics in Longevity Science

### The Scale of Animal Use

The Interventions Testing Program (ITP) — the NIA-funded gold standard for longevity drug testing — uses thousands of genetically heterogeneous UM-HET3 mice per intervention, with cohorts of 150–400 mice per sex per site, across three sites simultaneously. The most recent ITP paper available on PubMed:

**Snyder JM et al. (2026).** "End-of-life pathology in UM-HET3 mice treated with 16α-hydroxyestradiol or late-start canagliflozin." *GeroScience* 48(2):1787-1797. **PMID: 40601216**
- Authors: Snyder, Harrison, Korstanje, Ginsburg, Reifsnyder, Nelson, Leiser, Kaczorowski, Imai, Salmon, Strong, Ladiges, Miller. All IACUC-approved.
- Illustrates the scale: full life course studies on hundreds of mice per cohort, examining end-of-life pathology.
- Ethical disclosure: "All animal studies were performed as part of an approved IACUC protocol at their respective institutions."

### The Ethical Issues Specific to Longevity Research

The peer-reviewed animal ethics literature does not directly address ITP-style longevity research as a specific subfield. However, several issues arise:

1. **The lifespan study problem.** Standard 3Rs (Replace, Reduce, Refine) principles in animal ethics require justification for animal use. Lifespan studies that require monitoring animals until natural death over 2–3 years are among the most animal-intensive research designs possible. The justification is that lifespan is the only endpoint that cannot be inferred from shorter proxy measurements with confidence.

2. **Negative results and animal sacrifice.** ITP has found that many promising interventions (e.g., 17α-estradiol, canagliflozin) have sex-specific effects — increasing lifespan in males but decreasing it in females, or vice versa. Snyder et al. (2026, PMID: 40601216) is specifically examining why canagliflozin and 16α-hydroxyestradiol harm female mice. This reveals a structural problem: animals die in experiments that ultimately show harm, not benefit, for some subjects. The ethical justification requires that even null or harmful results provide scientific value.

3. **Translation failure.** The history of longevity research (resveratrol, GDF11, many others) involves repeated failure to translate mouse findings to humans. If the primary beneficiaries of longevity research are humans, but the research requires thousands of mice per intervention, and most interventions fail to translate, then the utilitarian calculus of animal use is challenging to justify. This is not a claim that ITP is unethical, but that the field needs explicit ethical accounting of translation rates.

4. **No species-specific PubMed bioethics literature.** Searches for "longevity research" + "animal ethics" or "ITP" + "ethics" in PubMed return no dedicated bioethics papers. This is a genuine gap in the literature. The ethical oversight is handled through IACUC processes (regulatory compliance) rather than philosophical analysis.

## 9. Public Attitudes Toward Life Extension

**Aparicio A (2024).** "Public alignment with longevity biotechnology: an analysis of framing in surveys and opinion studies." *Biogerontology* 26(1):13. **PMID: 39585500** (Review)
- Meta-analysis of public opinion surveys on anti-aging science over two decades.
- Key finding: Studies often "emphasize life extension over healthspan gains" in their framing, which affects public responses. When surveys ask about "living longer," public enthusiasm is mixed. When surveys ask about "staying healthy longer," enthusiasm is much higher.
- Implication: The public is not primarily motivated by longevity per se, but by avoiding age-related suffering. This has implications for how ethicists and scientists should communicate about anti-aging research — framing it as healthspan extension may be more socially legitimate than framing it as lifespan extension.

**Aparicio A (2024).** "Life Extension Should Come with Wisdom: Reflections and Questions for the Geroscience and Longevity Community." *Rejuvenation Research* 27(4):137-142. **PMID: 38877805**
- Companion paper. Argues that the geroscience community has insufficiently grappled with the social, psychological, and philosophical dimensions of extending human life.
- Calls for the longevity science community to engage seriously with what a much longer, healthy life should look like — not just the biology, but the social structures, meaning systems, and ethical obligations.

## 12. Synthesis: The Current State of the Debate

**Where consensus exists:**
1. The "naturalness" objection to anti-aging is philosophically weak. Bioconservative arguments have largely lost ground in the academic literature.
2. Overpopulation as an objection to slowed-aging technologies specifically (not radical immortality) is empirically weak (Wareham 2015).
3. Equity/access is the strongest genuine ethical concern. Most ethicists who support anti-aging research also argue it must be paired with universal access frameworks.
4. The ICD-11 extension code XT9T is a significant but partial victory for the "aging as disease" position. It enables epidemiological tracking but does not unlock drug approval pathways.

**Where genuine disagreement persists:**
1. Whether the treatment/enhancement distinction can or should be maintained for aging. Masny (2023) argues it should be dissolved on justice grounds; others maintain it as practically necessary for resource allocation.
2. Whether physicians have a duty to prescribe gerotherapeutics when available (McKiddy 2025 argues for cautious integration with equity safeguards).
3. The moral status of the "complete life" concept: Does justice require that everyone get a complete life as currently understood (several decades), or is the concept itself transformable by new technologies?
4. Animal research: No dedicated ethical analysis of ITP-scale longevity research exists. This is an unaddressed gap.
