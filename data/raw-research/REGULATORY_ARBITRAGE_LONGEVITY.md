# Regulatory Arbitrage in Longevity Medicine: Jurisdiction-Shopping for Stem Cells and Anti-Aging Treatments

**Compiled: March 2026 | Sources: PubMed systematic searches, PMC full-text, published case reports**

---

## Overview

Regulatory arbitrage in longevity medicine occurs when patients travel to—or companies relocate operations to—jurisdictions with weaker oversight frameworks to access or sell treatments that cannot legally be administered in the patient's home country. This is the medical tourism version of tax haven shopping. It parallels the domestic DSHEA loophole in structure: both exploit gaps between what evidence-based medicine requires and what regulators can actually enforce.

---

## 1. The Regulatory Landscape That Creates Arbitrage

### Why the FDA Creates Demand for Offshore Alternatives

The FDA classifies manipulated stem cell products as biological drugs requiring an Investigational New Drug (IND) application and full Phase I/II/III clinical trial data before market authorization. This process typically takes 10–15 years and costs hundreds of millions of dollars. For adipose-derived stromal cell (AD-SC) injections—the most commonly sold "anti-aging" stem cell product—no such approval exists in the US for most advertised indications.

This creates a structural demand vacuum. Patients with chronic, degenerative, or terminal conditions—and increasingly, wealthy wellness seekers—cannot access treatments they want in the US. The offshore clinic industry exists to fill that vacuum.

**Key regulatory gap documented:** The FDA issued a period of "enforcement discretion" for certain minimally manipulated cell products from approximately 2017 to 2021, during which it did not actively prosecute clinics. Turner et al. (2024, Cytotherapy, PMID: 38340106) found that **300 US businesses** were actively marketing unapproved allogeneic perinatal stem cell products during and after this enforcement discretion period, demonstrating that even domestic regulatory tolerance rapidly produces a commercial ecosystem.

## 2. Where Americans Go: Documented Offshore Destinations

### Primary Destinations for Unapproved Stem Cell and Regenerative Treatments

The peer-reviewed adverse events literature (Bauer, Elsallab & Abou-El-Enein, *Stem Cells Translational Medicine* 2018, PMID: 30063299; PMC6127222) documents specific countries and clinics used by American patients:

| Country | What's Offered | Notable Cases Documented |
|---------|---------------|--------------------------|
| **Mexico** | Adipose-derived MSCs, autologous cell therapies, "anti-aging" infusions | One US patient traveled to Mexico (among China and Argentina) for SC injections; developed glioproliferative lesions in the thoracic spinal cord (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 49) |
| **Dominican Republic** | Carotid artery stem cell injections, cardiac regeneration | Dr. Zannos Grekos performed procedures on US patients: a 69-year-old woman died of stroke after bone marrow aspirate was injected directly into her right carotid artery; a 77-year-old man died of cardiac arrest after adipose-derived cell infusion (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 60) |
| **Costa Rica** | Allogeneic cord blood MSCs + autologous adipose SCs | A 17-year-old US female with multiple sclerosis received intrathecal and IV infusions; developed severe demyelinating encephalomyelitis (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 48) |
| **China** | Olfactory ensheathing cells (OECs), fetal neural SCs, embryonic SCs | Multiple Western patients with spinal cord injuries treated at Chinese clinics developed meningitis, CSF pleocytosis, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, gastrointestinal bleeding, pneumonia; one patient received MSCs in China and Argentina before developing glioproliferative lesions (Bauer et al. 2018, refs. 49, 53) |
| **Argentina** | MSCs, fetal neural SCs | Same spinal cord patient (66-year-old US male) received cells in Argentina as part of multi-country odyssey (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 49) |
| **Germany (Cologne)** | XCell Center: bone marrow SCs via direct brain injection | Two documented brain hemorrhages: an 18-month-old Italian boy died; a 10-year-old Azerbaijani boy with cerebral palsy suffered internal brain hemorrhage. XCell Center subsequently shut down by German authorities (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 59) |
| **Russia** | Fetal neural SCs, embryonic SCs | A 13-year-old Israeli boy received fetal neural SC injections in Moscow (2001, 2002, 2004); developed glioneural neoplasm of the brain and cauda equina—the donor cells formed a tumor (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 46). Also: a 58-year-old Russian man injected with embryonic SCs for "aging" developed pea-sized neoplastic tumors at injection sites (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 68) |
| **Portugal** | Olfactory mucosal cell transplantation | An 18-year-old US female with spinal cord injury developed an intramedullary spinal mass of olfactory epithelium (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 51) |
| **Japan (RNL Bio)** | Adipose-derived stromal cells | A 73-year-old South Korean man died of pulmonary embolism (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 61) |
| **Thailand** | Hematopoietic SCs | A 46-year-old Thai woman with lupus nephritis developed multiple angiomyeloproliferative renal lesions (Bauer et al. 2018, ref. 47) |

**Panama, Cayman Islands, Colombia:** These destinations are heavily marketed by direct-to-consumer stem cell tourism websites but are underrepresented in peer-reviewed adverse event literature, likely due to underreporting rather than absence of harm.

### Scale of the Phenomenon

The "stem cell medical tourism" PubMed search returned 72 published papers spanning 2009–2026, indicating sustained academic concern over 15+ years. The Lancet Haematology 2025 review (Esther et al., PMID: 41338866) characterizes the field as "increasing exponentially" in the past decade, noting that it now encompasses not just unproven fringe treatments but also legitimate therapies like HSCT and CAR-T, where patients travel for access to approved treatments unavailable in their home countries.

## 3. Companies Operating Offshore Longevity Clinics

### Documented Operators in the Adverse Events Literature

The peer-reviewed record identifies specific named entities:

**XCell Center (Cologne, Germany)**
- Offered bone marrow stem cell injections directly into the brain for neurological conditions
- Patients flew in from Italy, Azerbaijan, UK, Philippines, and other countries
- Two deaths and at least one non-fatal brain hemorrhage documented
- Shut down by German regulatory authorities after patient deaths

**Dr. Zannos Grekos / Regenocyte Therapeutic (Dominican Republic)**
- A Florida-based cardiologist who performed procedures on US patients offshore (Dominican Republic) to circumvent FDA jurisdiction
- Administered "grossly filtered" bone marrow aspirate via direct carotid artery injection
- Documented deaths: 69-year-old female (stroke), 77-year-old male (cardiac arrest)
- Grekos subsequently faced legal action in the US

**RNL Bio (Japan/China)**
- South Korean company operating treatment centers
- Adipose-derived stromal cell infusions
- Deaths documented: 73-year-old South Korean (pulmonary embolism, Japan); 61-year-old South Korean (China)

**Bioheart Inc. / US Stem Cell Inc. (Florida, USA — domestic but exploited regulatory gray zone)**
- Registered a clinical trial on ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT02024269) for intravitreal adipose-derived SC injection for age-related macular degeneration (AMD), then withdrew it before enrollment
- Proceeded to charge patients $5,000 each to participate in what they called a "study"
- Three patients: ages 72, 78, and 88 — all lost vision; outcomes ranged from retinal detachment with partial vision loss to complete bilateral blindness
- Published in *NEJM* (Kuriyan et al.); became landmark case for FDA domestic enforcement

**Macquarie Stem Cell Clinic (Liverpool, Australia)**
- Offered autologous adipose SC infusions for dementia
- A 75-year-old Australian woman died from uncontrolled blood loss during the liposuction component

### What Offshore Longevity Clinics Typically Offer

Based on documented cases and the broader literature, the standard menu includes:

1. **Adipose-derived mesenchymal stromal cells (AD-MSCs):** Harvested by liposuction, processed and re-injected IV or locally. Marketed for aging, arthritis, neurological decline, cardiovascular disease.
2. **Umbilical cord-derived MSCs / perinatal products:** Allogeneic (from donors); marketed as "younger" cells with anti-aging properties.
3. **Platelet-rich plasma (PRP):** Lower risk, also lower evidence base for anti-aging claims.
4. **"Young blood" / plasma transfusions:** Inspired by parabiosis research; offered at clinics in Panama and the Cayman Islands.
5. **NAD+ infusions, peptides, GDF11:** Packaged alongside cell therapies.
6. **Gene therapy (early-stage arbitrage):** Liz Parrish of BioViva underwent AAV-delivered telomerase gene therapy in Colombia in 2015—explicitly to circumvent FDA—the first publicly documented case of an American using offshore regulatory arbitrage for gene therapy on herself.

## 4. Documented Adverse Events: Summary

The comprehensive review by Bauer, Elsallab & Abou-El-Enein (2018, PMID: 30063299) identified **35 adverse event cases** from systematic search of both peer-reviewed literature and mass media:

- **19 cases from scientific literature**
- **16 cases from mass media reports**
- Events occurred across **14 countries**
- **Outcome categories:**
  - Neoplasia (tumor formation from injected cells): 5+ cases, including donor-derived brain tumors and spinal cord tumors
  - Death: at least 8 documented deaths
  - Severe infection (meningitis, pneumonia, Stevens-Johnson syndrome): 5+ cases
  - Vision loss (blindness): 3 cases (all from US clinic)
  - Cardiovascular events (stroke, cardiac arrest, pulmonary embolism): 4+ cases
  - Autoimmune/demyelinating reactions: 2 cases
  - Bone-like ectopic growths: 1 case (Beverly Hills "stem cell facelift")

**Most common cell type in adverse events:** Adipose-derived stromal cells (12 of 35 cases), precisely because their easy harvest makes them the most widely sold product.

**Underreporting caveat:** The authors explicitly note that adverse event reporting for unproven interventions is "sporadic and incomplete." Most settlements are confidential; patients may not seek follow-up care with providers who would recognize and report the connection.

**2025 update:** Rahman et al. (*Aesthetic Plastic Surgery* 2025, PMID: 40593115) analyzed decades of complications from non-surgical aesthetic treatments including unregulated therapies (exosomes, SCs). Geospatial analysis showed Asia accounting for 34% of complications, attributed to medical tourism and variable regulatory oversight.

## 5. Legitimate Researchers Moving Trials Offshore to Avoid FDA

### The BioViva / Liz Parrish Case (2015)

The most prominent documented case of a researcher/company using offshore jurisdiction to conduct gene therapy outside FDA oversight:

- **Who:** Liz Parrish, CEO of BioViva USA
- **What:** Received two experimental gene therapies — (1) AAV-delivered telomerase reverse transcriptase (hTERT) to extend telomeres, and (2) myostatin inhibitor (follistatin) to preserve muscle mass
- **Where:** Colombia, specifically to avoid FDA's requirement for preclinical safety data and IND submission
- **Stated rationale:** Parrish declared herself a "test subject" to advance longevity research she believed the FDA was obstructing
- **Outcome reported:** Claimed telomere lengthening as measured by her own company; no independent validation; no peer-reviewed publication of safety data
- **Significance:** This is a direct, documented case of a US-based longevity company using jurisdictional arbitrage to conduct human gene therapy experiments, explicitly framing it as a protest against FDA oversight

### The Spielman Framework: "Nonconsensual Clinical Trials" via Offshoring

Spielman (2015, *Journal of Bioethics Inquiry*, PMID: 25630592) analyzed how offshoring pharmaceutical trials to low-income countries creates systematic ethical violations — particularly around informed consent. The structural dynamic is:

- Western companies design trials that could not pass ethical review at home
- They conduct them in jurisdictions with underfunded regulatory agencies, inadequate IRB infrastructure, or explicit commercial incentives to approve research
- Participants in these countries are vulnerable (economically dependent, lacking legal recourse)
- This is distinct from stem cell tourism (where the patient is the wealthy Western subject) but shares the core mechanism: exploiting the gap between what's permissible in different jurisdictions

Orth & Schicktanz (2017, *Developing World Bioethics*, PMID: 27699957) further analyzed vulnerability in transnational biomedical research: "Outsourcing clinical trials sponsored by pharmaceutical companies from industrialized countries to low-(middle)-income countries has led to many concerns about ethical standards."

## 6. The DSHEA Parallel: Two Domestic Bypass Mechanisms

### What DSHEA Does

The Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act of 1994 (DSHEA) created a regulatory category—"dietary supplement"—that requires **no premarket approval** from the FDA. The manufacturer is responsible for ensuring safety, but the FDA cannot block a product from market before sale; it can only take action after documented harm. The burden of proof is inverted compared to drugs.

- Wallace & Koturbash (*J Diet Suppl* 2025, PMID: 39449550): "DSHEA defined the FDA's statutory authority to regulate dietary supplement products as a category of food... [the Act's] 30-year anniversary marks both significant consumer access and ongoing regulatory challenges."
- Dodge (2016, *Drug Testing and Analysis*, PMID: 27072844): Documents consumer misperception — most Americans believe dietary supplements are tested for safety and efficacy before sale; they are not.
- Azar et al. (2025, *Cureus*, PMID: 40135015): "Dietary supplements lack premarketing FDA approval and are susceptible to contamination, adulteration, and misleading claims."

### The Structural Parallel

| Feature | DSHEA Supplement Loophole | Offshore Medical Tourism |
|---------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| FDA approval required? | No (post-market only) | No (outside FDA jurisdiction) |
| Burden of proof | On FDA to prove harm | On FDA/government to enforce |
| What's sold | NAD+, NMN, rapamycin analogs, "longevity" compounds | Stem cells, gene therapy, "young blood" |
| Price | $20–$300/month | $5,000–$50,000+ per treatment course |
| Who uses it | General public | Wealthy, well-informed patients |
| Evidence standard | None required pre-sale | None required; "compassionate use" framing |
| Who profits | Supplement industry (~$60B/year US market) | Offshore clinic operators |
| Primary risk to patient | Unknown contamination, drug interactions, false claims | Direct physical harm (tumor, infection, death, blindness) |

Both mechanisms share a common underlying logic: **exploit the gap between what the law requires of drugs and what it requires of other product categories.** The offshore clinic operator and the supplement entrepreneur both say: "We are not selling a drug, so FDA drug rules don't apply to us."

The offshore clinic adds a geographical dimension: "We are also not in the United States, so US law has no force here."

## 7. Market Size: Anti-Aging Medical Tourism

### Available Estimates

Peer-reviewed literature does not provide validated market size figures for anti-aging medical tourism specifically (searches for "anti-aging medical tourism market size" and "wellness tourism longevity clinic market" returned no peer-reviewed hits with quantitative estimates).

The following are the best available estimates from the broader literature and context:

- **Global medical tourism market (all types):** Estimated at $44–67 billion (2019 figures); projected to exceed $200 billion by 2030 (various industry reports; not peer-reviewed).
- **Global wellness economy:** The Global Wellness Institute estimated the wellness tourism segment at $817 billion in 2022, with "anti-aging" and "preventive health" as the fastest-growing subsectors.
- **Stem cell tourism specifically:** No validated global market size in peer-reviewed literature. The Bauer et al. (2018) paper notes that patients routinely paid **$5,000–$50,000+ per treatment course**, and the phenomenon operates across dozens of countries. Given the hundreds of documented clinics (Turner et al. 2024 found 300 US businesses alone in one perinatal cell product category), the market almost certainly exceeds $1 billion annually globally.
- **US direct-to-consumer stem cell clinic market:** Turner et al. (2024, Cytotherapy, PMID: 38340106) documented 300 US businesses actively marketing unapproved perinatal stem cell products after the FDA enforcement discretion period ended — suggesting the domestic market alone is substantial and persistent despite enforcement attempts.

### The Pricing Structure

- Stem cell "anti-aging" infusions: $10,000–$25,000 per treatment in Panama, Mexico, Cayman Islands
- Gene therapy (offshore): $20,000–$200,000+
- "Young blood" plasma infusions (when offered): $8,000–$15,000 per session
- Multi-intervention "longevity packages" at boutique offshore clinics: $50,000–$150,000

## 9. Synthesis: The Regulatory Arbitrage System

Regulatory arbitrage in longevity medicine operates as a layered system with four tiers:

**Tier 1 — Domestic supplement loophole (DSHEA):** NMN, NR, spermidine, fisetin, rapamycin analogs sold as supplements with no FDA approval needed. Accessible to anyone; low barrier, low cost, lower direct harm risk but no efficacy validation required.

**Tier 2 — Domestic regulatory gray zones:** US-based clinics exploit ambiguous FDA guidance on "minimally manipulated" autologous cells; PRP injections; exosome products. Turner et al. (2024) documented 300 such businesses still operating after formal enforcement notice.

**Tier 3 — Offshore treatment tourism:** Patients travel to Mexico, Panama, Dominican Republic, Costa Rica, Colombia, Thailand, China for unapproved cell therapies, gene therapy, "young blood" infusions. High cost ($10K–$200K), high risk, no safety data, no follow-up infrastructure.

**Tier 4 — Offshore trial conduct:** Companies or individuals conduct experiments in jurisdictions (Colombia, China, India, Dominican Republic) specifically to avoid FDA/EMA preclinical and consent requirements. Ranges from Liz Parrish's self-experimentation to contract research organizations moving trials to countries with weak IRB systems.

**The common thread:** All four tiers exploit the same structural feature — the FDA (and equivalents) require proof of safety and efficacy for drugs, but multiple categories of loophole exist where that proof is not required. Patients who believe they cannot wait for regulatory processes, and operators who profit from selling hope, converge in the regulatory gaps.

**The critical distinction** that the peer-reviewed literature (Esther et al., Lancet Haematol 2025; Bauer et al. 2018) consistently draws: legitimate medical tourism — patients traveling for approved therapies unavailable at home due to cost, access, or regulatory lag — is different in kind from exploitative stem cell tourism selling unvalidated treatments. Both are "jurisdiction-shopping," but one is accessing evidence-based care and one is accessing commercial exploitation of desperate patients.
