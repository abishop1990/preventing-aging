# Regulatory Reform Proposals for Aging Interventions

**Research date: March 2026 | Sources: PubMed, PMC, institutional websites**
**PubMed searches conducted:** Three original searches + 20+ follow-up searches

---

## Overview

The regulatory problem is not abstract. It is the rate-limiting step for the entire field. The science has advanced faster than the regulatory and funding infrastructure can accommodate. This document catalogs concrete, specific proposals — some in active pursuit, some theoretical — to reform the landscape.

---

## 1. Has Anyone Formally Proposed FDA Accept "Aging" as an Indication?

### Current Status: Informal Advocacy, No Formal Petition

No formal Citizen Petition or New Drug Application has been filed with the FDA asking it to accept "aging" or "biological aging" as a standalone indication (as of March 2026). What exists instead is a sustained, coordinated advocacy and scientific infrastructure-building effort.

### The Geroscience Hypothesis Framework

The most influential formal articulation is the **geroscience hypothesis**: that biological aging is the common driver of multiple age-related diseases, and that by targeting aging biology, it is possible to delay or prevent multiple conditions simultaneously. This is the scientific foundation for any future aging indication.

Key foundational paper: Barzilai N, Crandall JP, Kritchevsky SB, Espeland MA. **"Metformin as a Tool to Target Aging."** *Cell Metab.* 2016 Jun 14;23(6):1060-1065. **PMID: 27304507** (PMC: 5943638)

### The TAME Regulatory Wedge Strategy

Rather than directly challenging FDA's classification of aging as not-a-disease, the field chose a surrogate strategy:

**TAME (Targeting Aging with Metformin)** was designed specifically to create a regulatory template:
- Primary endpoint: **composite of time to first new diagnosis of CVD, cancer, cognitive impairment/dementia, or death** — not "aging" itself
- FDA accepted an Investigational New Drug (IND) application, marking the **first time FDA formally engaged with a trial that explicitly targets aging biology as its mechanism**
- The TAME team explicitly stated this was designed to "lay a regulatory foundation for future FDA approvals"

Design paper: Justice JN, Niedernhofer L, Robbins PD, et al. **"Development of Clinical Trials to Extend Healthy Lifespan."** *Cardiovasc Endocrinol Metab.* 2018 Dec;7(4):80-83. **PMID: 30906924** (PMC: 6428447)

**What TAME success would mean:** If TAME demonstrates that metformin delays the multi-disease composite endpoint, it would create a **regulatory template** — a demonstrated pathway for drugs targeting aging biology. Subsequent drugs would not be starting from zero in negotiations with FDA. The composite multimorbidity endpoint could become accepted practice. This would not make aging a "disease," but it would make "preventing age-related multimorbidity" a legitimate drug indication.

**Current status (as of March 2026):** TAME began enrollment across 14 academic sites in 2023. Target: ~3,000 participants, ages 65-79 without major chronic disease. Funded by the American Federation for Aging Research (AFAR), NIA, and the **Hevolution Foundation**. Expected completion: approximately 2027-2028 for primary data.

### What It Would Actually Take for FDA to Accept an Aging Indication

Based on the published literature and FDA workshops, the requirements would include:

1. **Validated surrogate endpoint for biological aging** that predicts clinical outcomes — currently none is FDA-qualified (see Section 3)
2. **Demonstrated that a drug changes the surrogate endpoint** — CALERIE showed DunedinPACE responds to intervention (PMID: 37118425), but this is not yet FDA-qualified
3. **Demonstrated that changing the surrogate endpoint causes clinical benefit** — the highest bar; requires either a very large trial or an accepted correlate
4. **Or alternatively:** TAME succeeds with a hard clinical composite, and then that composite becomes accepted as the "aging indication" proxy

The WHO ICD-11 compromise (Extension code XT9T: "Ageing-related"; and MG2A: "Ageing-associated decline in intrinsic capacity") provides epidemiological infrastructure but does not bind FDA. An influential discussion of whether this opens a path is contained in: Khaltourina D et al. 2020. *Mech Ageing Dev.* **PMID: 32251691**.

The Bhasin/FDA workshop described the regulatory problem explicitly: Bhasin et al. 2023 (*J Gerontol A*, **PMID: 37325959**) documented an FDA-participatory meeting explicitly stating that "lack of regulatory guidance has impeded development of function-promoting therapies."

## 2. Biomarker Qualification Pathway: DunedinPACE and GrimAge

### FDA's Biomarker Qualification Program

Under the 21st Century Cures Act and 21 CFR Part 610, the FDA Biomarker Qualification Program (BQP) allows stakeholders to submit evidence for a biomarker's qualification as a drug development tool. Requirements include:
- Analytical validity (reproducibility, reliability)
- Biological plausibility
- Clinical validation (predicts outcomes in populations)
- Intervention responsiveness (changes with treatment in RCTs)
- Defined context of use

### Status of DunedinPACE: Strongest Candidate, No Formal Submission

**DunedinPACE** (Pace of Aging Computed from the Epigenome) is currently the leading candidate for an FDA-qualifiable aging biomarker:

- Measures the rate of biological aging from blood DNA methylation (not just biological "age" at a point in time)
- Among the strongest single predictors of mortality in longitudinal cohorts
- **Critically: Shown to respond to randomized intervention** — the only epigenetic clock to demonstrate RCT responsiveness

Key RCT paper: Waziry R, Ryan CP, Corcoran DL, et al. (Columbia/Duke/NIA teams). **"Effect of long-term caloric restriction on DNA methylation measures of biological aging in healthy adults from the CALERIE trial."** *Nat Aging.* 2023 Mar;3(3):248-257. **PMID: 37118425** (PMC: 10148951)

The CALERIE trial found: Caloric restriction slowed the **pace of aging as measured by DunedinPACE** (effect size ~0.02 years of biological age per calendar year, equivalent to ~2% slowing over 2 years), while **not** significantly changing biological age estimates from other clocks (GrimAge, PhenoAge). This is a pivotal finding: DunedinPACE responded to intervention; the static clocks did not.

**The validation gap for FDA qualification:** The CALERIE result established intervention responsiveness, but the FDA would also require:
- Demonstration that a drug that changes DunedinPACE actually changes clinical outcomes (mortality, multimorbidity) — the "surrogate-to-outcome" link
- This requires a trial with both DunedinPACE and hard clinical outcomes as co-endpoints — which TAME is positioned to provide (DunedinPACE is a secondary biomarker in TAME)

**No Biomarker Qualification Submission has been filed with FDA for DunedinPACE or GrimAge as of March 2026.**

The Delphi expert consensus (Kritchevsky et al. 2025) found that **28 geroscience experts agreed that blood-based biomarkers, including epigenetic clocks, would be unlikely to be accepted as primary endpoints of efficacy trials under current FDA standards.** They remain candidates for secondary endpoints and accelerated approval-supporting data.

TAME Biomarkers Workgroup framework: Justice JN, Ferrucci L, Newman AB, et al. **"A framework for selection of blood-based biomarkers for geroscience-guided clinical trials: report from the TAME Biomarkers Workgroup."** *Geroscience.* 2018 Dec;40(5-6):419-436. **PMID: 30151729** (PMC: 6294728)

### GrimAge and GrimAge2

**GrimAge** (based on training against time-to-death) and its successor **GrimAge2** are the strongest predictors of all-cause mortality among epigenetic clocks. GrimAge2 hazard ratio for all-cause mortality exceeds 2.5. However:
- GrimAge does not show clear RCT responsiveness (CALERIE showed no significant GrimAge change with caloric restriction — only DunedinPACE moved)
- Without intervention responsiveness, it cannot serve as a surrogate endpoint for drug trials

Vyas et al. 2023 used GrimAge as an exploratory endpoint in cognitive decline (PMID: 37212116, *J Alzheimers Dis*), demonstrating longitudinal association but not intervention responsiveness.

### Practical Timeline for FDA Biomarker Qualification

A realistic sequence:
1. **2027-2028**: TAME completes, provides DunedinPACE-to-multimorbidity linkage data
2. **2028-2030**: Stakeholder consortium (AFAR + Hevolution + NIA + academic centers) assembles Biomarker Qualification Submission for DunedinPACE
3. **2030-2033**: FDA review process (typically 2-3 years for BQP submissions)
4. **~2033 earliest**: DunedinPACE potentially qualified as an aging trial endpoint for drug development, enabling shorter Phase 2 aging trials

This timeline could compress significantly if TAME data is unambiguous and FDA engages proactively.

## 3. Adaptive Platform Trials: Could a RECOVERY-Style Trial Work for Aging?

### The RECOVERY Model Applied to Aging

The RECOVERY trial for COVID-19 enrolled ~40,000 participants and tested ~10 interventions simultaneously using adaptive design, providing definitive answers on dexamethasone, tocilizumab, baricitinib, and others — within months rather than years. Could an analogous platform trial work for aging?

**Structural answer: Yes, but with fundamental differences.**

The RECOVERY trial had:
- A single clear endpoint (28-day mortality)
- Rapid disease progression (results within weeks)
- Population cohort assembled by emergency (hospitalized COVID patients)

An aging platform trial faces:
- Composite multi-disease endpoint requiring 5+ years of follow-up
- No validated fast biomarker endpoint (yet)
- Diverse healthy aging population that must be enrolled prospectively
- Higher baseline drug interaction complexity in elderly populations

### The Proposed TAME-Platform Extension

The TAME investigators explicitly described TAME as a **first step in developing a geroscience trial template** that could extend to other compounds. Justice et al. 2018 (PMID: 30906924) states:

> "We outline features that could be more broadly extended to other geroscience-guided clinical trials"

The envisioned next-generation platform would test multiple geroprotective agents (senolytics, NAD+ precursors, mTOR inhibitors, etc.) simultaneously against the same composite aging endpoint, with participants able to be randomized to any arm. The TAME composite endpoint (CVD + cancer + dementia + death) would serve as the shared platform endpoint.

### Adaptive Platform Design for Geroscience: Key Requirements

Based on expert consensus (Kritchevsky et al. 2025, PMID: 39989956) and the ICFSR framework (Cesari, Bhasin et al. 2022, PMID: 35441189):

**Endpoint requirements for a platform trial:**
- Must include **multiple health dimensions**: age-related disease onset, physical function, and patient-reported outcomes
- Must be **tailored to population characteristics** — frail vs. robust elderly require different endpoints
- Plausible composite elements: mortality, mobility function (gait speed, SPPB), and first onset of 2+ age-related diseases
- **Blood-based biomarkers will NOT be accepted as primary endpoints** (Delphi consensus) but can be mechanistic and secondary

**Regulatory considerations:**
- Each arm of a platform trial still requires an IND for the compound being tested
- Adaptive designs require pre-specified adaptation rules (FDA Guidance: "Adaptive Designs for Clinical Trials of Drugs and Biologics," 2019)
- FDA has accepted adaptive platform trial designs in oncology and infectious disease; no geroscience-specific guidance exists yet

**The basket trial alternative:** A basket design (testing one drug across multiple age-related diseases simultaneously) is potentially easier to execute than a full platform trial and might provide faster regulatory data. Example: Testing a senolytic against three conditions simultaneously (osteoarthritis, IPF, and frailty) under a single master protocol.

The ICFSR task force report (Cesari, Bhasin, Fielding et al. 2022, PMID: 35441189) documents the regulatory discussion from a September 2021 FDA-adjacent workshop, specifically noting that preliminary results of multiple pharmacological candidates (myostatin inhibitors, activin receptor ligand traps, GLP-1 receptors in frailty) are under evaluation but none have been approved for frailty or sarcopenia.

## 4. The Hevolution Foundation: Transforming the Funding Landscape

### What Hevolution Is

**Hevolution Foundation** (Riyadh, Saudi Arabia) is a non-profit foundation backed by the Public Investment Fund (PIF) of Saudi Arabia. Founded approximately 2020-2021, led by Dr. Mehmood Khan (former Mayo Clinic and PepsiCo executive). It is the **largest dedicated funder of geroscience research globally** by budget.

Khan M. **"Funding Life-Extension Research."** *Cold Spring Harb Perspect Med.* 2023 Apr 10;13(12):a041208. **PMID: 37037599** (PMC: 10691464)

This paper, written by Hevolution's CEO, is the primary published articulation of its strategy. Key points:

- **Mission:** Fund "moonshot" geroscience research that traditional sources (NIH, pharma) will not fund because of high failure rates and long timelines
- **Diagnosis of the problem:** Traditional NIH funding favors low-risk, near-term incremental science; pharma requires patent protection and commercial viability; neither supports large geroscience trials with off-patent drugs
- **The ARPA-H model applied to aging:** Hevolution explicitly draws on ARPA-H (Advanced Research Projects Agency for Health) as an inspiration — risk-tolerant, outcome-focused, long-term
- **Capital scale:** Estimated $1 billion/year in grants and investments, making it comparable to all NIA geroscience-dedicated funding

### Hevolution's Key Investments

**Primary grant: TAME trial.** Hevolution provided significant new funding to unblock TAME enrollment after years of funding gaps. Without Hevolution, TAME would likely still be waiting for funding in 2026.

**Grants program:** Open grants to academic investigators for geroscience research, including basic aging biology, biomarker development, and trial infrastructure. Dozens of grants to NIA-affiliated investigators, Buck Institute, Einstein, Mayo Clinic, and international centers.

**Investments program:** Early-stage investments in longevity biotech companies — specifically companies developing aging targets that lack commercial viability under standard pharma models.

**Open Longevity Prize:** Hevolution co-sponsors prizes for validated aging interventions, drawing on the XPRIZE model.

### Why Hevolution Changes the Landscape

Khan's paper is explicit: the field is entering its "translational phase" and the geroscience hypothesis will be confirmed or refuted by RCTs. But this requires:
- Willingness to fund trials that will "fail" (non-commercial interventions, off-patent compounds)
- Long time horizons (10+ year trials)
- Integration across basic science, biomarker qualification, trial design, and regulatory strategy

Without Hevolution, the TAME trial — the only trial ever designed to test the geroscience hypothesis in humans — would have been indefinitely delayed. The funding gap is not rhetorical; it is documented in multiple publications noting that TAME spent years assembled but not funded because no pharma company would support a metformin trial.

**Structural concern:** Hevolution is a Saudi sovereign wealth fund-backed foundation. Its sustainability, independence from political interference, and alignment with academic research norms are questions that have been raised privately in the geroscience community but not yet directly addressed in the peer-reviewed literature.

## 5. Legislative Proposals

### ARPA-H and Aging

The **Advanced Research Projects Agency for Health (ARPA-H)** was authorized under the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2022 and began operations in 2022-2023 with initial funding of ~$1 billion. ARPA-H was explicitly modeled on DARPA's high-risk/high-reward structure.

Collins FS, Schwetz TA, Tabak LA, Lander ES. "ARPA-H: accelerating biomedical breakthroughs." *Science.* 2021; 373:165-167. (Cited in Khan 2023, PMID: 37037599)

Aging research is within ARPA-H's mandate. As of early 2026:
- Several ARPA-H programs address age-related diseases (cancer, neurodegenerative disease, immune aging)
- No ARPA-H program has been announced specifically for "aging as a target" or geroscience-hypothesis testing as of the research date
- The ARPA-H model (unlimited funding authority, hiring non-civil-servant PMs, large direct contracts) could in principle fund a RECOVERY-scale aging platform trial

**The DOGE complication (2025-2026):** Under the Department of Government Efficiency initiative, NIA's budget has been under pressure. NIA geroscience program funding is uncertain for FY2026-2027. If NIA aging research funding is cut, Hevolution's role becomes even more critical — and the dependence on a single Saudi foundation for the field's flagship trial becomes a structural vulnerability.

### The "Longevity Dividend" Congressional Push

S.J. Olshansky and colleagues have repeatedly argued for a federal "longevity dividend" investment, noting that Goldman et al. 2013 (*Health Affairs*, PMID: 24101058) calculated a $7.1 trillion economic return over 50 years from a 2.2-year healthspan extension. This has not resulted in specific legislation.

There is no specific bill in the 119th Congress (2025-2026) dedicated to aging as a disease classification or FDA reform for geroscience, as of March 2026.

**The Healthy Aging Act (prior Congress):** Previous congresses have seen bills addressing healthy aging, but these focused on geriatric care workforce, not regulatory reform for aging drugs.

**The RIGHT Act (Research Investments in Geriatric Health and Equity) concept** has been discussed in aging policy circles but not introduced as legislation.

### FDA Safety Modernization Act Implications

Von Haehling et al. 2025 (*J Cachexia Sarcopenia Muscle*, **PMID: 40485364**) documented a December 2024 FDA workshop on sarcopenia drug development. FDA stated that its safety requirements for sarcopenia drugs would match diabetes trial standards — meaning programs "so large that from an economic standpoint only therapies that significantly impact on morbidity/mortality outcomes have a chance to be considered commercially feasible." This is FDA explicitly describing the regulatory barrier as structural, not resolvable by scientific innovation alone.

This statement implies that **legislative action to create a dedicated regulatory pathway for geroprotective drugs** — analogous to FDA's orphan drug designation or the breakthrough therapy designation — may be necessary. Such a pathway has been proposed in the literature but not yet enacted.

## 6. The Post-TAME Regulatory World: What Happens If TAME Succeeds?

### Scenario Analysis

#### If TAME Meets Its Primary Composite Endpoint

"Success" means statistically significant reduction in time to first composite event (CVD + cancer + dementia + death) in participants randomized to metformin vs. placebo over 6 years.

**Regulatory implications:**
1. **Metformin cannot be FDA-approved for "aging"** because metformin is off-patent. FDA approval requires a sponsor with commercial interest. No sponsor exists.
2. **What TAME success actually creates:** A demonstrated composite endpoint for geroscience trials. FDA has now seen a trial test this endpoint type and found it meaningful. Future drugs targeting aging biology can use a similar endpoint in their IND/NDA applications.
3. **Off-label prescribing will accelerate.** Physicians will begin prescribing metformin to healthy elderly more aggressively. Currently ~333 documented cases of off-label aging use (Kaeberlein et al. 2023, PMID: 37191826); this number would likely expand substantially.
4. **Insurance coverage might follow.** If TAME shows mortality benefit, some insurers might cover metformin for the multimorbidity indication (it is already on most formularies for diabetes).
5. **Platform trials become fundable.** TAME success is a proof-of-concept that de-risks future platform trials for investors and funders. ARPA-H, Hevolution, and pharma with patentable next-generation compounds all gain a roadmap.

**What TAME success does NOT do:**
- Does not create an FDA indication for "aging"
- Does not qualify any aging biomarker
- Does not lower the bar for future aging drug approvals

#### The DunedinPACE Route to Accelerated Approval

If TAME also shows that metformin slows DunedinPACE significantly, and if that DunedinPACE change correlates with clinical outcomes, then the **case for FDA's Accelerated Approval pathway** becomes plausible for future aging drugs:

- **Accelerated Approval (21 CFR 314.500)** allows approval based on a validated surrogate endpoint "that is reasonably likely to predict clinical benefit"
- A drug could potentially be approved faster based on its DunedinPACE effect if FDA qualifies that endpoint
- This is the "Holy Grail" path: a 2-year Phase 2 trial showing a drug slows biological age → accelerated approval → post-marketing 5-year confirmatory trial → full approval

Realistic timeline for this path: 2033-2040 for first drug approved via an aging biomarker endpoint.

#### The Composite Endpoint as "Aging Indication" by Stealth

The TAME composite (CVD + cancer + dementia + death) essentially *is* what most people mean by "aging." If FDA accepts it as a valid endpoint in one trial, it has created an *ad hoc* aging indication without ever formally classifying aging as a disease. This is the most politically achievable path and the path the field is deliberately pursuing.

## 7. Novel Trial Designs for Geroscience

### A. The Kritchevsky-Bhasin Delphi Framework (2025)

**The most current and authoritative consensus document on geroscience trial design:**

Kritchevsky S, Zamora E, Bhasin S, Cohen A, Chandler A, Covinsky K, Crane H, Cummings S, Erlandson K, Espeland M, Espinoza S, Ferrucci L, Fleming A, Forman D, Justice J, Kuchel G, Mannick J, Newman A, et al. **"Selecting Appropriate Clinical Trial Endpoints for Geroscience Trials: A Path Towards Consensus."** *Res Sq [Preprint].* 2025 Feb 12. **PMID: 39989956** (PMC: 11844647)

**Method:** Modified Delphi process, 28 experts from clinical trials, geriatrics, geroscience, and regulatory translation. Four focus groups, then a structured survey with >66% threshold for consensus.

**Key consensus findings:**

1. Outcome measures MUST include **multiple health dimensions** — age-related disease onset, physical function, AND patient-reported outcomes reflecting participant goals
2. Endpoints MUST be **tailored to population characteristics** — the right endpoint for "robust" older adults differs from frail elderly
3. **Blood-based biomarkers (including epigenetic clocks) are UNLIKELY to be accepted as primary endpoints** of efficacy trials under current FDA standards
4. **Plausible composite primary endpoint components:**
   - Mortality (all-cause)
   - Mobility/physical function (gait speed, SPPB, grip strength)
   - First onset of multiple age-related diseases (2 or more of: CVD, cancer, diabetes, COPD, dementia)
5. No full consensus emerged — experts disagreed on relative weighting of function vs. disease
6. **Regulatory translation** identified as a key gap: no specific FDA guidance on multi-disease aging trial endpoints exists

**Notable participant detail:** Alexander Fleming (Kitalys Institute, former FDA Division Director) and Joan Mannick (formerly of Life Biosciences/Tornado Therapeutics, lead PI of the RTB101 rapalog aging trials) were among the 28 experts. Their inclusion signals that regulatory and industry perspectives are embedded in this consensus process.

### B. The ICFSR/Bhasin Framework for Sarcopenia-Frailty Drug Development

Cesari M, Bernabei R, Vellas B, Fielding RA, Rooks D, Azzolino D, Mariani J, Oliva AA, Bhasin S, Rolland Y. **"Challenges in the Development of Drugs for Sarcopenia and Frailty — Report from the International Conference on Frailty and Sarcopenia Research (ICFSR) Task Force."** *J Frailty Aging.* 2022;11(2):135-142. **PMID: 35441189** (PMC: 9017069)

This report (from September 2021 FDA-adjacent workshop) provides the most specific regulatory framework for the closest disease-adjacent geroscience indication:

**Key recommendations:**
- Sarcopenia and frailty endpoints must include **physical performance measures** (SPPB, 6-minute walk, stair climb power) AND muscle mass AND patient-reported outcomes
- The **SPPB (Short Physical Performance Battery)** is the leading candidate functional endpoint — it predicts hospitalization, disability, and mortality
- COVID-19 pandemic introduced remote assessment technology (wearables, video assessment) that could enable larger trials with lower per-participant cost
- **No pharmacological agent has been approved for frailty or sarcopenia** anywhere in the world as of 2022 — this is the gap the framework is trying to close

**Candidates under evaluation at time of this report:**
- Myostatin inhibitors (Acceleron/Merck: bimagrumab; Eli Lilly: LY3002813)
- Activin receptor ligand traps
- Testosterone/SARMs (Bhasin's own area)
- GLP-1 receptor agonists (for muscle preservation in obesity-related sarcopenia)
- Senolytics (dasatinib+quercetin, fisetin)

### C. The COMPASS-T Framework

Multiple NIA-funded research networks (including the Pepper Centers and the Claude D. Pepper Older Americans Independence Centers) have been developing consensus trial infrastructure:
- Shared data repositories for trial biomarkers
- Harmonized physical function assessment protocols
- Recruitment pipelines through geriatric practice networks

This "plumbing" work — not visible in a single landmark paper — enables future platform trials by establishing the operational infrastructure needed to run multi-site, long-duration aging trials efficiently.

### D. NAD+ Augmentation as a Model Geroscience Trial

The Bhasin laboratory at Harvard has run the most rigorous geroscience-guided trial series for a non-TAME target:

Pencina KM, Valderrabano R, Wipper B, Orkaby AR, Reid KF, Storer T, et al. **"Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide Augmentation in Overweight or Obese Middle-Aged and Older Adults: A Physiologic Study."** *J Clin Endocrinol Metab.* 2023 Jul 14;108(8):1968-1980. **PMID: 36740954** (PMC: 10281506)

This trial (MIB-626, a patentable NMN formulation) illustrates the trial design strategy:
- Use a patentable formulation of an off-patent compound to create commercial incentive
- Target a specific physiologic endpoint (NAD+ levels, energy metabolism) rather than aging directly
- Build biomarker data to support larger trials

MIB Biosciences (Bhasin's company) is developing this as the commercial path for NAD+ augmentation. This is the "disease-wedge via patentable formulation" strategy applied to an otherwise supplement-market molecule.

## 8. Integrated Assessment: The Reform Agenda

### What Would a Functional Regulatory System for Aging Drugs Look Like?

Drawing on all the above, a minimally functional regulatory system would require:

**Step 1 — FDA Qualification of DunedinPACE (or equivalent) as a surrogate endpoint**
- Required action: Stakeholder consortium files Biomarker Qualification Submission
- Required evidence: TAME data linking DunedinPACE to multimorbidity outcomes
- Timeline: ~2030-2033
- Barrier: No stakeholder has yet been designated to lead the qualification submission process

**Step 2 — FDA Guidance on Composite Multi-Disease Aging Endpoints**
- Required action: FDA issues formal guidance document (following a Guidance for Industry process)
- Required evidence: TAME success + Delphi consensus framework (PMID: 39989956)
- Timeline: ~2029-2032
- Barrier: Requires FDA internal champion; no current FDA official publicly committed to this

**Step 3 — A Dedicated Regulatory Pathway (Breakthrough-Aging Designation)**
- Required action: Congressional legislation or FDA administrative action creating a new designation (analogous to Breakthrough Therapy Designation) for drugs targeting aging biology
- Timeline: Dependent on political will — could be fast (1-2 years) with the right sponsor or never
- Barrier: "Aging is not a disease" classification is the structural obstacle; legislators who can articulate economic ROI are essential here

**Step 4 — A Funded Geroscience Platform Trial**
- Required action: Hevolution + NIA + ARPA-H jointly fund a RECOVERY-scale platform trial testing 5-8 geroprotective candidates simultaneously against the TAME composite endpoint
- Budget: ~$500M over 10 years
- Timeline: Could begin 2028-2030 if TAME data is available and political will exists
- Barrier: NIA budget pressure under DOGE; Hevolution's political sustainability

## 10. Gaps in the Literature — What Is Not Yet Formally Proposed

The following specific items were searched for but **do not yet exist** as formal published proposals (as of March 2026):

1. **No published Citizen Petition to FDA asking it to accept aging as a disease** — the field has chosen the TAME regulatory-template strategy instead of direct confrontation
2. **No Biomarker Qualification Submission filed** for DunedinPACE, GrimAge, or any aging clock
3. **No Congressional bill** specifically addressing FDA regulatory reform for aging drugs (the approach has been scientific, not legislative)
4. **No published design paper** for an explicit RECOVERY-style geroscience platform trial — this exists only as a concept in symposium discussions
5. **No EMA counterpart** to the TAME regulatory-wedge strategy — European geroscience lags US in trial infrastructure
6. **No formal "Breakthrough Aging Designation" proposal** to FDA — discussed informally by Kitalys Institute and others but not formally submitted
